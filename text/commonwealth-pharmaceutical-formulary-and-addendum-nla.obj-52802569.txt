PREFACE.
COMMONWEALTH PHARMACEUTICAL FORMULARY.
In compiling the Commonwealth Pharmaceutical Formulary,
the Formulary Committee has concerned itself with the necessity
for providing a range of drugs sufficiently comprehensive to satisfy
the demands of contemporary therapeutic practice. To this end
a detailed analysis has been made of all the more important current
pharmacopoeias and an examination undertaken of relevant medical,
pharmaceutical and allied scientific literature. In several instances it
has been necessary to evolve new formulae, to meet the requirements
of modem practice. Such formulae, however, have been included
only after a thorough technical investigation of the problems raised.
A few drugs have been excluded from the Formulary on the
ground that their use is based more on tradition rather than upon
any consideration of medicinal value. On the other hand, taking
cognizance of an increasingly wide field of new and complex thera-
peutic agents, the Committee has sought to include only those whose
claim to recognition is founded on grounds other than those of
mere novelty. At all times, nevertheless, it has been the Committee’s
aim to provide a full range of those drugs which are of established
therapeutic worth.
While due regard to economy in prescribing has been observed,
in no case has therapeutic efficiency been subordinated to any
consideration of drug economy. An examination of the Formulary
will show that any economy exercised has been directed solely
towards the elimination of redundant ingredients such as superfluous
flavouring and colouring agents.
In the section headed “ Notes for the Guidance of Medical Prac-
titioners in Using the Commonwealth Pharmaceutical Formulary ”,
information is set down and examples are cited to indicate how
formulae may be modified so as to ensure flexibility and individuality
of treatment where these are required.
It is unnecessary to state that there are many difficulties in pro-
curing certain drugs at the present time and that it is impossible
to forecast the available stocks of any drug in the near future. In
compiling the Formulary the committee has not concerned itself
with these problems, but has included all drugs that are considered
necessary to meet the normal medical needs of the community.
This policy was adopted in order that the public should bo able
to take full advantage of existing stocks, and any stocks that may
become available from time to time. The only other alternative
would have been to exclude from the Formulary drugs likely at
any time to bo in short supply. This would have considerably
restricted the scope of the Formulary, and at the same time would
debar the public from the benefits of the Pharmaceutical Benefits
Act when and where stocks become available.

Pharmaceutical benefits under the Pharmaceutical Benefits Act
consist of such uncompounded medicines, medicinal compounds,
materials and appliances as are provided for by the Commonwealth
Pharmaceutical formulary and the Addendum thereto.
Every person ordinarily resident in the Commonwealth is entitled
to pharmaceutical benefits, provided that—
(a) He receives from a medical practitioner a signed prescrip-
tion, written on the prescribed form.
( b) He presents his prescription to an approved pharmaceutical
chemist where such is available.
In order to provide for the individual requirements of the patient,
the prescriber may vary any formula in any of the following ways :
(а) By reducing the quantity of any ingredient—e.g. first
write the title of the formula and then indicate the
reduced dose of the ingredient (or ingredients),
e.g. Mist. Chloral, et Brom.

—e.g. indicate in the directions the dose required,
e.g. Mist. Calc. Chlor.

(d) By adding any drug specified in the list of allowable
additions (see Creams, Mixtures, Pastes and Ointments)

dose.

(e) By increasing the quantity of any specified ingredient

(/) By using a formula for a purpose other than that indicated
in the Formulary. Thus Gargarisma Boracis Composita

The prescriber’s attention is drawn to the fact that it is not
permissible to increase the quantity of any ingredient of a formula,
unless the ingredient is marked *.
All formulae should be prescribed under their full or abbreviated
titles. It is not necessary to include the formula in detail.
All prescriptions must be written in ink or in a case of emergency
with an indelible pencil and signed with the full signature of the
medical practitioner. A rubber stamp must not be used.
Directions in regard to “repeats” must be in the handwriting
of the prescriber.
It is desirable that the patient’s name should be written in block
letters.
In the case of most mixtures the Formulary sets out an average
dose. As the prescriber may increase or decrease the dose he
should always indicate in the directions the dose to be taken.
It is important that only one prescription should be written on
each prescription form.
Prescription forms must not be used except to prescribe such
pharmaceutical benefits as are provided under the Pharmaceutical
Benefits Act.
Standards. —All drugs, chemicals and preparations supplied in
accordance with the provisions of the Pharmaceutical Benefits Act
shall conform both in regard to purity and composition to official
standards.
Where the abbreviations 8.P., B.P.C. and A.P.F. are used they
indicate the standard formulae of the following works :
B.P. —The British Pharmacopoeia and its Addenda.
B.P.C.—The British Pharmaceutical Codex and its Supplements.
A.P.F. —The Australian Pharmaceutical Formulary.
Ointment bases, nevertheless, may bo varied in regions where the
prevailing temperature renders the specified base unsuitable. The
active ingredients, however, must conform to the requirements of
the prescriber.


All drugs and chemicals used in the preparation of any formula
must also conform to official standards where such exist. Whore
no standard is provided they must be of a quality not lower than
the grade or quality ordinarily used for medicinal purposes.
Repeat Prescriptions.—Medical practitioners may order any
proscription to be repeated up to the number of times indicated
under the title of each typo of preparation, providing the quantity
ordered is the maximum quantity available.
Where it has been deemed advisable to prescribe a lesser quantity
than the maximum available, the direction covering repeats does
not apply.
The presoriber must indicate in words over his signature the
number of times the prescription is to bo repeated,
