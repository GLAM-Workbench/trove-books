FOREWORD
By Lieutenant-General S. G. Savige, C. 8., C.8.E., D. 5.0., M.C., E.D.
THE publication of this photographic record will be appreciated by all of us
who wore the "Red and White Diamond" color patch, because it illustrates the path-
way of Service trod by the men of the 24th Bn. in the war against Japan.
Valuable as this book is in these days immediately following the war, it will be-
come more valuable in future, as it will provide a key to unlock the treasure house of
rich memories covering our days of Service and Sacrifice.
As we turn its pages, we shall see ourselves as we were in those memorable
days. It will recall associations with fine fellows, which created comradeship in the
Field, and cemented a friendship tasting through life. We shall pause, here and
there, as we recognise a perl killed, either at Markham Point, in the advance along
the Buin Road, or elsewhere in the field. It will revive memories of many a kindly
act bestowed upon us by comrades when the going was tough. It will remind each
of us, too, that we are better men because we endeavoured to do likewise.
May every success attend this splendid effort on the part of those responsible
for its publication.

w HEN Singapore fell, among those taken prisoner by
the Japs, were many Indian troops, many of them later being
brought to New Guinea as labourers. How many were
brought will probably never be known. But twenty-nine of
these prisoners will long remember the 24th Bn. and one
of its N.C.O.'s —Sgt. Gregory, M.M,
These twenty-nine Indian P.O.W.'s escaped from the
Japanese during the advance by the 6th Bde. in the Sepik
River area.
Reports concerning them were received by the Bn. in June,
1944 —reports that showed they were in bad shape.
Food and instructions were flown in to them at SANAI, Then
Sgt. Gregory was flown in with R.A.P. supplies.
Sgt. Gregory not only had to treat the
sick and wounded Indians, but also
had to recruit native labor to build an
emergency landing strip from which
the unfit could be flown out.
In addition, he organised the defence
of his charges against the probing pat-
rols of the Japanese, although only
trained in medical work. At his re-
quest arms and ammunition were
dropped to him from a U.S.A.A.F, Fly-
ing Fortress.
For his sterling work in this rescue
venture Sgt. Gregory was awarded the
M.M.
The Indians demonstrated their grati-
tude by giving invaluable information
of the Japanese forces.

and Unit records, and any errors or omissions which may have
occurred are regretted. It also contains the names of members
transferred to A.A.0.C., A.E.M.E., A.A.P.C. and A.A.C.C.
Decorations shown are those awarded up to 30th June, 1945 only,
and do not include awards of C.-in-C. and G.O.C.'s Cards.
