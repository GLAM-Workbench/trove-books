PREFACE.
Ik preparing the following Catalogue of Plants for the Press, it occurred to
me, that, by a small additional outlay, it might be rendered useful as a book
of reference to those of my fellow-colonists who take an interest in the
study of plants and horticulture.
The rapid progress which this delightful and useful science has made,
within the last few years, renders the publication of some work of easy
access, at the present moment, very desirable.
Although there are, doubtless, many scientific gentlemen in the colony
more competent for such an undertaking than myself, yet a beginning must
be made ; and it is in the hope that this beginning, small although it be,
may be quickly followed by more complete productions from the pens of
those better qualified, that I have ventured on the task.
It must be observed, in the first place, that the Catalogue is nothing
more than a nurseryman’s list, made subservient to the particular end in
view; and that its highest aim is the conveying of information that has
never hitherto been published in so condensed a form; it, however,
includes nearly all the cultivated, ornamental, and fruit-bearing plants, at
present in the colony.
The system employed by Dr. Lindley, in his “ Vegetable ’Kingdom,”
and funded on the natural affinities of plants, has been followed in the
arrangement, as the most complete and intelligible ; but any supposed
advantage that may belong to the alphabetical arrangement is supplied by
the index.
The authorities for genera are given in the usual contractions, which are
supposed to be familiar to all botanical readers, and, therefore, need no
explanation in the present instance.
The distinction of hardy from tender plants, it is hoped, may be found
useful to horticulturists in all the Australian colonies ; and in this distinc-
tion, for which I am entirely responsible, I have been guided partly by
experience and partly by considerations derived from comparisons, &c. ;
it may, therefore, be supposed that in some instances I am in error,
but taken as a whole, I feel confident that it will be found comparatively
correct.


In describing the habits of plants, as shrubs, bulbs, herbaceous, &c., I
have been guided by their usual station in gardens, and by the mode of
cultivation, rather than by their strict botanical characters.
The short practical observations on the cultivation of the Orange and the
Olive, by which the catalogue is followed may, I trust, be the means of
turning the attention of the well- wishers of this highly favoured colony to
these most important subjects.
That this humble attempt may be received in the same spirit with which
it is made ; and that it may be followed by others more brilliant and more
useful for the advancement of science, and thereby of the wealth and
the happiness of Australians, is the sincere wish of their friend and
fellow-countryman,
T. W. SHEPHERD.
Darling Nursery, Sydney,
Ist July, 1851.

A FEW PRACTICAL REMARKS ON THE CULTIVATION
OF THE
ORANGE AND THE OLIVE.
THE ORANGE.
If is unnecessary, in these remarks, to enter into calculations on the exportable value
of this fruit; it is sufficient to say, that a large sum is annually added to our income
from the demand already existing in the neighbouring colonies; and that as these
colonies become more densely peopled the demand must proportionately increase.
It is therefore the evident interest of this colony to turn its attention to the better
cultivation of a fruit so suitable to its climate, and so certain to be a source of large
profit.
At present, our Orangeries, with a few exceptions, are badly cultivated; while,
at the same time, no tree sooner responds to proper treatment, or sooner declines by
the contrary ; and it is the writer’s aim now to supply such information as will enable
the colonists to cultivate them with success.
Soil.—A strong loam approaching to clay and free from iron-stone is the most
suitable, and should be of considerable depth, say from two to three feet, but if
deeper all the better. The subsoil must not be too retentive of water —such as rock
or stiff clay—a broken, slightly porous, or stony subsoil, is best suited for such a
purpose, as it will allow the superfluous moisture to drain off, and the soil above
neither gets too wet nor too dry. It is a well ascertained fact that drained land
retains moisture better than that which is not drained, and liable to become saturated
with wet. Fertility of soil is not of so much importance in the first instance as a
proper substratum ; for the former may be supplied by manure, but the latter is not so
easily managed. Of all fruit trees the orange is most susceptible of injury from bad
drainage.
Situation. —Gentle slopes, sheltered from prevailing winds, either hot or cold, with
northerly or easterly aspects, are the best calculated for orange plantations ; flat land
is the most unsuitable, unless it consists of a good, deep, and permeable soil. If the
locality be a cold one the plantation should not be in valleys, or low situations, as the
trees will not endure much frost. /
Preparation op Land,- —-No planting should be proceeded with until the whole of
the land has been trenched to an uniform depth ; the proper depth depends upon
that of the soil; two feet will generally be found sufficient, but no bad soil
must be brought to the surface. In trenching, the land should. not be broken too
fine, as it is more liable to bind than when turned up rough ; and if possible it should
be trenched two or three months prior to the planting season.
Planting. —The best season for this operation is immediately after the March and
April rains ; but it frequently happens that we have no rain in either of these months ;
in this case it is better to await the spring ; nor should it be proceeded with during
severe frosts. After the land has been prepared it should be laid out in the manner
it is intended to be planted, by placing a stake where each tree is to stand; the holes
should not be opened until immediately before planting, nor when the ground is so
wet as to adhere to the spade. The proper distance from tree to tree, for strong
growing kinds, is twenty feet in the rows, and twenty-five or thirty feet row from
row ; varieties of dwarf habits may be placed closer. It is not necessary to open the
holes more than is required to cover the roots two or three inches deeper than they had


previously been, which can be seen by the earth mark round the collar or base of the
stem; all long, straggling, and tap roots should be cut in. In covering the roots if
u S i°iV be Very h ‘T sh , and lum Py> some of a san dy nature mixed with rotted manure
should be procured a few spades full of which will be sufficient for each plant: a
little water, say half a gallon, should be poured upon it, so as to settle the soil well
about the roots ; a much larger quantity would be injurious. It is better not to head
down the young orange trees until the roots have taken hold of the soil - and care
should be taken to preserve the foliage, as the leaves perform a very important part
m assisting the formation of young roots, especially in early planting. After planting
each tree should be supported by one or more stakes driven firmly into the "round to
prevent too much shaking by strong winds. In large plantations each variety should
have its separate station; as, if mixed indiscriminately, the labour of gathering the
fruit will be much increased. 8 8
Selection of Plants.— lt is of the greatest importance that healthy and vigorous
plants should be procured; for when weakly and unhealthy they remain dormant for a
long time, and seldom recover. Grafted or budded trees are preferable to any other

Seville orange. It is a complete waste of time and money to plant any other than
worked trees. Although a good variety may be obtained from seed, yet in most
cases a worthless one will be the result; and the great length of time before seedlings
bear fruit renders the practice still more objectionable. One year old plants if
sufficiently strong, are the most likely to succeed; if two years old they ought to
have been headed down, and removed the previous season; each plant should have
a clear straight stem of at least two feet in height, and if branched at the top, short
jointed wood should be chosen.

qualities are solidity, weight, compactness, and a smoothness ofrmd, combined with
good flavour and keeping qualities ; for the latter, size and flavour are the prin-
cipal considerations. Some of our finest oranges are not good keepers, such are the

The Bahia, or Navel Orange, is much esteemed in this colony for its superior
flavor and size; it was introduced from Bahia in Brazil more than twenty years a"o
and is now cultivated in almost every orangery. The fruit may be known by the
singular protruding scar at its apex, whence it derives the name “navel ” bv which it

Great care should be taken to procure the varieties wished tor ; it is as expensive to


cultivate rubbish as the primest fruit; no tree should be planted unless there is a
certainty that they are actually what they are represented to be, otherwise dis-

Diseases. —The orange tree is subject to various diseases, the most prominent of
which are the scale, and the thrip; the former being by far the most common in this
colony; it is a mistaken notion that its appearance is caused or increased by the
small black ant, which is always found in large numbers upon the trees infected; the
cause of their presence is nothing more than their search for a sweet deposit made by
the insect, of which the scale is the covering. The disease itself, apparently, is more
the effect than the cause of bad health* for when the tree is made to grow freely it is
generally cast off, and while it continues to do so the scale is kept down; but as
soon as the tree gets into bad health from want of fertility in the soil, wet sub-
stratum, or other cause, the scale again makes its appearance. The best remedy is
manure in abundance, and if no improvement is perceptible after a year or two’s
trial, cut the tree out, and plant a fresh one in its place, for some constitutional
weakness in the tree itself must be the cause of bad health. If through neglect a plan-
tation has become completely overrun with scale, and bark-bound, the trees should
be sawn down in winter, within a foot or so of the surface, manure well supplied, the
stumps in the following spring will put forth young luxuriant shoots, and if the
manuring be kept up, hoeing attended to, and the site in the first instance has been
judiciously selected, they will continue healthy. The cause of the “thrip” is not so
easily understood; it often makes its appearance when the tree is otherwise perfectly
healthy, and again disappears without a perceptible cause. When a tree has be-
come very much affected by it, the most practicable remedy is to cut it down, as
before recommended ; but this should only be done in extreme cases.
Manure, &c.— Unless the soil is naturally very rich, too much manure can scarcely
be supplied ; it should be decomposed before use ; the particular kind is of little con-
sequence, provided it is calculated to enrich the soil; it should be dug in slightly.
All weeds should be kept down by the hoe, and the surface kept loose and mode-
rately rough, so as to enable it to receive all the benefit of every chance shower.
Multching is not desirable, except immediately after planting; for the roots make
for the surface in search of the temporary moisture arising therefrom, and when
the litter becomes dry, perish; the tree thereby receiving a check which otherwise
would have been avoided.
Concluding Observations. —ln the orange growing countries of Europe, the fruit
will hang upon the trees two and sometimes three years after ripening, by which it is
thought the flavour is improved; it rarely happens in this colony that the fruit
will remain six months on the tree after ripening, and the crops often prematurely
ripen. This is owing to the dryness of our climate. In very dry seasons it is found
that the trees become shrivelled in every part, and their health is injured so much
that they do not recover for a long period. This might be prevented by irrigation,
and there is little doubt that if the soils and situations previously recommended
could be accompanied by a sufficient supply of water for irrigation, oranges could be
grown here to as great perfection as in any part of the world. And it may yet be
found that the very dryness with which we have to contend can be turned to our
advantage in the cultivation of so desirable a fruit.
The cultivation recommended in the above remarks is applicable to all the species of
the citrus tribe.


THE OLIVE.
1 he suitableness of this climate for the successful cultivation of the olive, is beyond
a doubt; already small quantites of oil and pickled olives of very superior quality
have been manufactured, and there is little doubt that the quantity will yearly increase.
Several plantations have recently been formed, and there is every reason to suppose
that the proprietors will ere long reap a golden harvest from them.
When the small amount of outlay necessary for their formation, and the little care
and labour required in their cultivation is considered, it is a matter of surprise that
plantations have not more rapidly sprung up. Our imports of olive oil and fruit
amount to a considerable sum yearly, which would long before this have been
rendered unnecessary had the colonists been more enterprising in this matter. In
Greece and Italy, where the olive is largely cultivated, a few trees are sufficient
for the support of a family ; a full grown tree, under favourable circumstances, will
produce yearly sufficient fruit to make from twenty to thirty gallons of oil, and there
are instances of trees from whose crop, in one season, upwards of fifty gallons have
been made ; but such trees are of great age and very favourably situated. About
nine years after an olive plantation has been formed, it begins to pay, and every
succeeding year it increases in value, until half a century old, when it may be con-
sidered in full bearing, and in favourable situations will bear for ages.
It is extremely desirable that Colonists who may have it in their power should take
up this important subject, which, if carried out with spirit, cannot fail to prove highly
remunerative, not only to the individuals immediately concerned, but to the Colony
at large. The formation of an olive plantation is a simple and inexpensive under-
taking, the land requires little preparation, and until the trees have commenced to
produce, may be used for other crops.
Alluvial and somewhat calcareous soils are the most congenial to the growth of
the olive, the latter quality being necessary to impart to the oil the flavour and
character required; where soil of this nature exists to a good depth, with a loose and
permeable subsoil, a sloping surface, sheltered from high winds, and a distance not
too far from the sea, every natural advantage that can be wished for is obtained.
The olive tree will, however, accommodate itself to soils and situations far less
favourable; and there are few places in this colony, within fifty miles of the coast,
where undulating land and cultivable soil of a fair depth is to be found, that it could
not be profitably grown; but of course the more favourable the circumstances are, the
less the cost—and the more remunerating will be the cultivation.
If the land selected for a plantation be of an open and friable nature, trenching
may be dispensed with, deep ploughing will be sufficient preparation; on the other
hand, if it be of a close and binding nature, it will be useless waste to plant, with-
out first trenching the whole to an uniform depth of about two feet. After the
land has been prepared, the intended holes should be 'staked out, so as to ensure
uniformity in the plantation, the proper distance from tree to tree every way is forty
feet, but in the first instance they may be planted at half that distance apart, with
the intention of removing every alternate tree to a fresh plantation, when their
branches shall have met together ; which, under ordinary circumstances, they will do
in about fifteen years.
Planting may be proceeded with at any time between the months of April and
September, but May is considered the most favourable if the weather is moist; the
plants should not be less than three years old, and if older, all the better. In
removing strong trees it is advisable to head them down hard ; that is, cut them down
to within two or three feet of the collar, (or point of junction between root and
stem,) otherwise their growth will be much retarded; small trees should not be
headed down more than sufficient to prevent strong winds from shaking them
violently.
The olive may be propagated by cuttings, truncheons, suckers, layers, grafts, and
seed ; but it is only necessary in the present instance to notice those methods which
are considered the best and most suitable to the circumstances of the colony.
Truncheons are large cuttings, taken from the branches, of not less than two inches
in diameter, and cut into four feet lengths. They should be planted in trenches,
leaving two or three inches of the upper end above the surface. The soil for this


purpose should be light, friable, and sandy, and so situated that it may be kept
moderately moist; in three years they will be fit for the plantation. Winter is the
proper season to operate. This is undoubtedly the quickest mode for getting trees
into an advanced state, but it retards the trees which furnish the truncheons, whose
supply will for some time to come be limited. The next best method is grafting upon
seedlings of the common or wild olive, which are easily raised, and are fit for
grafting when the stem has attained the thickness of a man’s finger. August and
September are the proper months to proceed with this operation. The stocks should
have been removed, and the roots trimmed the previous season, to prevent too great a
check when finally taken to the plantation. Very good plants may be obtained from
what the Italians call uovoli, which are junks of the main root cut into pieces of
about three inches across, having the bark attached. To obtain these the tree must
generally be destroyed, and the plants so produced are little in advance of seedlings.
Suckers can only be obtained in limited quantities. Propagation by small cuttings
and layers is tedious, and need only be practised where the other methods are im-
practicable. In England, stocks of the common privet are sometimes used for grafting
upon, but there the tree is only grown for ornament, and it is not probable that the
practice will be found of any service where productiveness is the main consideration.
After the plantation has been made, it is only requisite to keep down the weeds.
Should the land be required for other crops until the olives commence bearing, the
tillage for such will be an advantage rather than otherwise. Such crops as do not
root deeply, and are calculated not to impoverish the soil to any great extent, are the
most suitable.
Olive oil is extracted from the pulp of the fruit, by presses or mills of simple
construction ; care must be taken that the seed is not crushed, otherwise the quality of
the oil would be injured. After the first pressing the marc is soaked in water and
fermented, when it is again submitted to pressure, and oil of an inferior quality to
the first or virgin oil is the result. The fruit for oil making should he gathered
in May, when it is just at maturity, and all decayed or decaying berries must he
carefully removed before pressing.
Pickled olives are prepared by steeping the unripe fruit in a lye composed of water
with a small quantity of quick-lime, and should be changed every twenty-four hours
for about three weeks, when pure water only, must be used for a few days, after
which they are ready for bottling in salt and water, to make them fit for use.
