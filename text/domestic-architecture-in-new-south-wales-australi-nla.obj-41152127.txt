FOREWORD
Emeritus Professor L. WILKINSON, F.R.1.8.A., F.R.A.I.A.
The late Dean of the Faculty of Architecture
at the
University of Sydney
If domestic Architecture is to progress, the public must be encouraged
to take an interest in it. There is evidence that such interest is being
awakened and on turning the pages of this book the reader will gain
a good idea of the kind of house that has been produced in and around
Sydney. Wisely placed on the site and related to the garden, these
houses show appreciation of the importance of convenient planning,
and consideration of aspect, prospect and climate conditions. Although
the work of one architect, the individuality of the client has not been
submerged, which may or may not have been advantageous in the
result. Here is good logical use of various materials, also clever
handling of the varied and sometimes unusual problems presented by
the owners who are the fortunate possessors of these liveable and
attractive living places.
LESLIE WILKINSON.
Page Five


EDITOR'S NOTE
BEFORE turning to the subject matter of this book, I ask the reader to
pause, in order that I may help him to view its contents with the eye of
an understanding critic.
The buildings illustrated are of the cream of F. Glynn Gilling s domestic
work which, incidentally, comprises some of the finest houses in New South
Wales, but they are far from being the whole of the cream; lack of space
and of suitable photographs having caused the exclusion of many excellent
buildings.
Having before him some of the acknowledged best of an architect s
work, the reader may easily feel a particular licence for the exercise of
the most exacting criticism; however, he should bear in mind the thought
that although the designs are attributed to the architect, he has not been
their unfettered author. Unlike the writer, the painter and the composer,
whose work may reasonably be classified as solely their own; the work
of an architect, for its fulfilment, demands a client, a builder and a site.
In the domestic field the client's artistic taste is of major importance. Should
it be under-developed, the architect must, with extreme tact, influence and
educate his client to such a standard that he actually insists upon good
architecture. It is improbable that the owners of all the houses and flats
described in this work possessed good architectural taste; therefore, it behoves
the critic to take count of the psychological efforts of the architect when
assessing the results produced.
The illustrations are too small for one to appreciate the skill of the
builders and their tradesmen; but they serve, at least, to enable one to see
whether, as it should, each house "belongs'' to its site, or if it is in any
way incongruous to it. To make a house belong to the site, to stand rest-
fully upon its contours and fit comfortably within its boundaries may have
made demands upon the skill of the architect not easily appreciated from
an examination of the illustrations.
The reader should make an effort to visualise the problems which have
arisen in each instance to appreciate fully the quality of the finished product.
E. LINDSAY THOMPSON.
