THE FIRST PRINTED BOOK.
The Gutenberg Bible and the First Folio of Shakespeare
rank as the two most important books in the world; the
Shakespeare Folio as the keystone of English literature, and
the Bible as the first book printed with movable types.
Book-lovers have, for centuries, reverenced the Gutenberg
Bible (sometimes called “ the Mazarin Bible ” after the copy
in Cardinal Mazarin’s library), and for generations it has been
regarded as the collector’s greatest prize.
The task of producing such a book was gigantic. From
the printing of single sheets and pamphlets, Gutenberg, in
1450, set to work on a large two-volume Bible of nearly 1,300
pages, and his technical difficulties can only be imagined.
But the result was astonishing, and the beauty of its pages
has never failed to delight book-lovers.
A record price was paid about ninety years ago by James
Lenox, the celebrated American collector, who gave for
a copy of the book. From that time the value has steadily
risen, and in 1926 a very fine copy realised over £20,000.
Few collectors can hope to possess a complete copy of the
Bible, and the opportunity of securing a single leaf is to be
welcomed. We have a perfect leaf (with the initials, etc., in
blue and red by a contemporary rubricator) bound, with a
printed foreword by A. Edward Newton, in black morocco.
The price of this is £BO. (Item 1).
In 1913-14 three hundred copies of a superb facsimile in two
volumes, with illuminations reproduced in gold and colours,
were issued in Berlin, after the example in the Royal Library.
We have a set bound in imitation of the fifteenth-century-
bound Fulda copy, in new condition, priced at £6O, (Item 2).


A SEVENTEENTH CENTURY AUCTIONEER.
Of late years the auctions of England and America have
received more and more attention from journalists. The
human element in them appeals to everybody, and has done
so in England for more than a century. The famous Rox-
burghe Sale is still a cherished tradition among English book-
lovers, and the original catalogues of the libraries of famous
men such as Johnson and Garrick are looked upon with
reverence. Few people realise how comparatively modern
English book auctions are. The earliest one on record took
place towards the end of the seventeenth century, and one of
the first men to devote themselves to this new trade was “ the
famous Mr. Edward Millington,” as John Dunton styles him
in his Life and Errors. He was “ a man of remarkable
elocution, wit, sense and modesty,” and kept his clientele in
a good humour by the exercise of it.
We have here a copy of the elegy which Dunton says was
written “ in his memory,” and a specimen of one of his sale
catalogues.
The Elegy upon the Lamented Death of Edward Milling-
ton, 1703 (which is a two-page broadside with uncut edges,
priced at £25 [item 3]) gives an interesting picture of a
seventeenth century auction and Millington’s amusing way of
auctioneering.
“ Come, Gentlemen, —come bid me what you please;
Upon my Word, it is a curious Piece,
Done by a Learned Hand—and neatly bound:
What say you?—come —Fie put it up, —One Pound;
One Pound—once, twice; fifteen : Who bids; a Crown:
Then shakes his Head, with an afected Frown,
And says, for Shame, consider, Gentlemen,
The Book is sold in Shops for more than ten.
Good lack a day!—’tis strange, then strikes the Blow,
And in a feigned Passion bids it go.”
The catalogue, a quarto of 54 pages, was issued by Milling-
ton in 1680. The sale commenced at 9 a.m. on 29th March,
and the afternoon session at 2 p.m. Bound in half morocco,
price £/\. [ltem 4].

£40,000 FOR A BIBLE
(From Our Own Correspondent)
* VIENNA.
The celebrated 42-line Gutenberg Bible
of the Austrian Benedictine Abbey of
St. Paul in Carinthia, which is in
possession of very valuable collec-
tions, is reported to have been sold
to America for $200,000 (£40,000). This
bible has a far greater value than that
formerly in the Austrian Monastery of
Melk, which a few months ago was sold for
$106,000 (£21,000) to an American anti-
quary, and is now in the library of Yale
University. For the former is printed on
vellum, not on paper only, is in an excel-
lent state of preservation, not cut, and still
in the original binding.
As far as is known only twelve Guten-
berg bibles printed on vellum still exist.
Austria now possesses only one, which is
in the National (formerly Court) Library in
Vienna. If the report is correct that the
bible of vSt. Paul has actually been sold to
America, altogether eight Gutenberg bibles
have so far been acquired by Americans.
