Introduction
1. GENERAL
The tern Delta Edo, suggested by Yfescott (1962), is applied
to the three small languages Epie, Engenni and Degema, which are
spoken in the eastern part of the Niger Delta (see map).
These languages have been little studied until recently.
The first discussion of them is probably that by Talbot (1932),
who discovered that Engenni and Degema were languages of the Edo
group. His account runs as follows:
T The Egenni on the western bank of the juju river of the
same name and the people of Bush Degama, Bush Abonnema, etc,,
formed for some, time an ethnographical puzzle, the solution of
which there seemed little chance of determining until time could
be found to work out their vocabularies. Later, however, it
transpired that these people were branches of the Sobo sub-tribe
of Edo, the nearest of whom are the Igabo, inhabiting a few towns
to the south of Abaw, while the bulk dwell some hundred miles to
the west. Their kinship and origin had previously been as unsus-
pected by themselves as unknown to others, and indeed was indig-
nantly denied by some, especially the Egenni, who proudly claimed
to be autochthonous. In all, they probably number under 20,000
souls. The clue to the riddle was first given by the chief of one
of the principal quarters of Joinkarama, who, in reply to some of
the questions which we never failed to put to some of the head-men
of each tribe visited concerning their customs and beliefs, replied;
’The name of our part of the town is Isua, Our people are
called Inedua, Onuso, who is the oame as Tamuno or Chi, created
our forefathers on this bank of the river, and we have lived
here ever since. We are of the same race as the Bush Degama and
Bush Abonnema people, whither we sent colonies.


"Now many of our people are Christians; hut in olden days,
besides Onuso, who was the great creator as well as the G-od of
the Sky and of Thunder, we also worshipped Onyiku, which was our
name for the Earth Goddess. This word has no connection with the
Iho Onye-Iku, meaning 'big man', Then, too, we worshipped Utepi,
a woman, juju, to whom we prayed for help on behalf of our yams,
children and herds, as also four good catches when fishing. We
always sacrificed to this juju when the river was. at its highest
and ready to fall, saying to Utepi; 'The water is now about to,
go down. Therefore look after us and our children. Let the
souls of the people not follow the.falling water and so be lost.'
"Besides all these, we worshipped our ancestors. When a
man's father died, he poured out drink to the spirit, saying;
'Asume, yi edudi' [ 'My father, come and drink']," It was this
invocation which supplied the first clue to the origin of the
people, and, although the chief declared over and over again
"Sobo are quite unknown to us", later information placed their
kinship beyound doubt,’
Talbot’s account is interesting in showing that the knowledge
of their connection with Edo peoples shown by the Engennl today
is a recent rather than an old tradition, and indicates that the
Delta Edo people have been in the Delta for a long time. This
is confirmed by a comparison of their vocabularies with those of
other Edo languages, which show Delta Edo to be a fairly closely-

and disliked by the people themselves, it is not surprising that

Not all of these words had, however, been added at the time that

The orthography is basically that used by Elaine Thomas in
publications of the Institute of Linguistics, with the following
exceptions;
1, Tone marks have been added: 'for high and w for downstep,
low being left unmarked. For convenience, forms cited from other
languages have been marked in the same way, although this confli-
cts with the normal practice for Igbo (where high is left unmarked)
and Yoruba (where mid is left unmarked), It further implies an
analysis making use of downstep, although Elaine Thomas (196?)
has shown that for Engenni there are advantages in one postulating
upstep instead.
2, All of the vowels of the ’lowered' set are dotted, whereas
in the orthography only the first vowel, of a morpheme is dotted
and the vowel harmony of the language ensures that all subsequent
vowels in the morpheme are lowered. This implies disjunctive
writing of underlying forms with rules for elision.
3, In Degema,•orthographic ng is being used for both /ng/ and
for [q], which is probably an allophone of /h/, occurring rarely.
In the wordlists, [33] is distinguished by being written Ag, For
the sake of consistency, the same convention has been followed in
Epie, where [$ ] is probably an allophone of /k/,
A comparison of these wordlists with those of other Delta
languages shows clearly that the Delta is a closely connected
culture area. Many words are common to a number of languages;
examples will be found in the lists under such items as cannon,
cast-net, mangrove, money, pig, thatch. Many of these are words


of the markets, which are centres of multi-lingual interchange
between groups of people who speak different languages hut who
share a considerable degree of common material culture. It is,
therefore, not always easy to know in which direction or via
what languages borrowing has gones but where there is apparently
a borrowing relationship involved, forms of other languages have
been cited* The presumption is that in most cases borrowing has
gone from the language cited to the smaller Delta Edo language.
The most frequent languages cited are Ijo and Igboj in the case
of the former, a good deal of dialect information is available,
and a form has been cited from a dialect which shows as close a
resemblance as possible to the Delta Edo form. Where several
Ijo dialects have forms equally close to the Delta Edo one, one
• • a
which is geographically close to the Delta Edo language has been
chosen. If the Ijo form is followed by ’etc.*, it means that
other dialects have similar forms; where this is not the case,
it is of course possible that the borrowing has been in the other
direction, from the Delta Edo language into Ijo, Igbo words
cited have been mainly taken from the comparative wordlists
published by Armstrong (1967); where they are common to several
of the dialects, listed there, no indication of the dialect is
given, but where only one of the five has the form, it has been
indicated.
When the Delta culture words have been identified, the remain-
ing vocabulary is clearly Edo, and is of particular interest to
the comparativist because of the isolation of these languages
from the influence of the larger Edo languages and particularly
of Bini, Furthermore, it seems likely that these languages are
conservative in their phonology and have not undergone the sound
changes which have produced the intricate phonological systems
of the better-known Edo languages. It is, for instance, much


more obvious that Epie idemu f tongue’ is cognate with Bantu *-deme
•• • •
than that Bini atfavhen is. The field of Comparative Edo is a very
rich one, and it is surprising that the Edo group of languages,
internally varied and geographically accessible, has not long ago
been given the attention which it deserves from both descriptive
and comparative linguists. It is hoped that this publication will
play a small part in drawing the attention of linguists to this
strangely neglected field.
Finally, we hope that this work will be helpful to the speak-
ers of these languages. In a multilingual community, it is right
that everyone should proud of his own language and should respect,
and to some extent understand, the languages of his neighbours.
All languages and all cultures have a contribution to make towards
human understanding and development; we hope this work will help
to show the contribution of these three languages.
