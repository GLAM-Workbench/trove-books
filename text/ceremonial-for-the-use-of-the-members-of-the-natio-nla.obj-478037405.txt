The proceedings of the Plenary Synod may be
distributed into:—
1. PUBLIC SESSIONS.
2. GENERAL CONGREGATIONS.
3. PRIVATE CONGREGATIONS.
4. COMMITTEES.
The Public Sessions.
These solemn Sessions are held in the Cathedral,
and are open to all the faithful.
All the Bishops, Secretaries, Theologians, Represen-
tatives of Dioceses, Heads of Religious Orders, and all
others who form part of the Plenary Synod, take part
in them, wearing their full ecclesiastical dress.
These Sessions generally begin and close with a
procession.


At the Public Sessions the Profession of Faith is
made, the Decrees are promulgated, the Acts of the
Synod are signed, and all other matters are transacted
at which the faithful are entitled to assist.
There will be five of these solemn Public Sessions; —
1. On Sunday, the 15th of November, when the
Synod will be formally commenced.
2. On the 18th, for the unveiling of the Vaughan
Memorial Window in the north gable of the
Cathedral. ’ *.
3. On the 22nd, for the promulgation of Decrees.
4. On the 25th, in commemoration of our deceased
prelates.
5. On the 29th, for the promulgation of Decrees
and the close of the Synod.
The Special Ceremonial for these Sessions will be
set forth in detail at a subsequent page.
The General Congregations.
The General Congregations are composed of the
Archbishops, Bishops, and Theologians, and all the
Clergy who may be entitled to have part in the
Plenary Synod. These Congregations are held in the
Sanctuary of the Cathedral.
All the Decrees, before promulgation in the Public
Sessions, are read aloud in the General Congregations;
and any of the Theologians or other representatives of
the Clergy may make their remarks on them. Should
the Bishops so desire, the Schemata or draft proposals
may be also read ; and one or other of the Theologians
may be called upon by any of the Prelates to give his
opinion thereon. The Bishops themselves do not take
part in any discussion in the General Congregations,
and the faithful are not permitted to be present at
them.


The Private Congregations.
The Archbishops and Bishops, with the officials of
the Synod, form the Private Congregations. In these
all the matters submitted to the Synod are freely and
fully discussed.
The Bishops sit in order of seniority alternately at
each side of the President’s chair, and each in turn
gives his opinion on the matter proposed. No Bishop
speaks more than once on the same subject without
the permission of the Synod.
On the less important occasions, the votes are taken
by the Bishops standing up who are in favour of the
proposed matter, and subsequently by those standing
up who are opposed to it.
On all important matters the votes are given in
order of seniority by secret suffrages, the papers being
collected by the Assistant Secretaries.
The Committees.
The object of the Cemmittees is to draft or reduce
to proper form the special matters assigned to them.
When the Committee have fully drafted a series of
Decrees on any subject proposed to them, such Decrees
are handed to the Secretary of the Synod, to be sub-
mitted to the Bishops in the Private Congregations.
The Committees vary in number as the Bishops may
deem expedient.
The Head Committee is composed of the Archbishops
and of two or more Bishops, with the Secretary of the
Synod. All matters intended for the consideration of
the Synod are, in the first instance, submitted to them,
and if found proper, are by them assigned to the
special Committee to which they belong. The Decrees


referring to Diocesan Visitation and other like matters
are drafted by them and submitted to the Private
Congregations.
The other Committees, which may be at least four
in number, are composed of Bishops and Clergy, all the
Theologians and others privileged to assist at the
Synod being assigned to one or other of these Com-
mittees. The Committees are appointed and the
members of each are elected by the Bishops at the
first Private Congregation.
The four mixed Committees may have the matters
assigned to them as follows :
O
1. De Fide, de fidei Professione, de fidei periculis
vitandis.
2. De iis quae spectant ad Sacramenta et ad Sacro-
sanctum Missae Sacrificium.
3. De Clero.
4. De Catholicae juventutis educatione primaria,
intermedia, et Universitaria.
For prayers to be said at all the Private and General
Congregations, see pages 7 and 8.
First Private Congregation.
The first Private Congregation will be held at the
Cathedral Presbytery on Saturday, the 14th of
November, 1885, at 10 o’clock.
At this first Congregation the officials of the Synod
are appointed, and the Decrees of their appointment
are duly signed ; also, the Committees are elected, the
order of procedure and the rule of life during the
Synod are sanctioned, and the usual formal Decrees are
ordered.

The versicles and prayers being terminated, all who
do not wear the sacred vestments at Mass, lay them
aside, and the President vests for the Mass, which is
solemnly celebrated, during preparation for which all
sit with mitres or caps, according to rank.
When the President proceeds from the throne to the
altar, all rise, the bishops with mitres, and return the
salute given by the President.
The bishops then, laying aside their mitres, assist at
Mass, observing, with the others present, all the rules
of choir, according to rank, viz.—they recite the
Confession, Kyrie, Gloria, Credo, Sanctus, and Agnus
Dei, without mitres. They receive the incense from
the deacon, without mitres, each having previously
invited the person next him to receive that mark of
respect before him.
They receive and give the kiss of peace, in the usual
manner. The mitres are worn 1. —after the incensation
of the altar, when the President receives his mitre,
before being incensed, till it is laid aside for the introit.
2.—Every time the bishops sit after recital of Gloria,
Creed, etc. The bishops kneel at the verse “ Veni
Sancti Spiritus,” without removing the mitres. 3.
After the sacred species are consumed, till the
Communion. 4.—When the President resumes the
mitre at the end of Mass.
Mass being concluded, the Celebrant at the throne
lays aside the sacred vestments appertaining to the
Holy Sacrifice, and takes those adapted for the session,
as before Mass. All then proceed in processional order
to the place appointed for the session, if it be remote
from the high altar ; otherwise they retain or take
their places, as marked out for each, except the
President, who descends from the throne, and places
himself before the altar, where, at the invitation of the
deacon, all kneel, the President at the faldstool, who,
with the prelates, retains the mitre.

usual. A similar ceremonial is observed in the remain-
ing Sessions, the Psalms and Gospels being those of
the preceding Sessions, taken alternately.
If any Session is prorogued from the morning to the
afternoon, the morning meeting breaks up by order of
the President, who imparts a simple benediction, with
the sign of the cross; then lays aside the vestments,
and retires as usual. The evening cermonies open with
Pontifical Vespers, after which follows the Session in
the usual manner.

The deacon invites, as usual, to mental prayer, which
being concluded, the prelate, or other person appointed
for the purpose, ascends the pulpit and chaunts the
acclamations, which are responded to by the whole
assembly, the prelates meanwhile wearfhg mitres, and
turning towards the Council.
Then the prelates, still wearing their mitres, advance
to receive the kiss of peace from the President, after
which the solemn pontifical blessing is imparted, and
the indulgences published.
The deacon then chaunts—
V. Recedamus in pace.
All answer—
R. In nomine Christi. Amen.
The vestments are then laid aside, and all retire,
accompanying the President to the episcopal residence!
