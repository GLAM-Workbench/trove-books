PREFACE TO THE SEVENTH EDITION
Since the publication of the Sixth Edition we have
faced, and are facing, many changes in pharmaceutical and
in therapeutic practice.
Of these, perhaps the most far reaching is involved in
the imminent introduction of a National Health Scheme,
which will necessitate the use of a standard formulary—
the Commonwealth Pharmaceutical Formulary (C.P.F.).
Such a formulary, when introduced officially, must
logically replace the Plospital Section of previous editions
of the A.P.F.—this section having been added originally
to the A.P.F. as a uniform basic standard for hospital and
contract dispensing.
However, in view of an unexpected delay in imple-
menting the National Health Scheme, the Editorial Com-
mittee has considered it inadvisable to delete the Hospital
Section from the present edition. It is therefore retained,
but with many alterations which will bring it into line
with the proposed C.P.F.
As the Commonwealth Pharmaceutical Formulary will
include many references to A.P.F. formulae, titles being
cited in many cases in lieu of the prescriptions, the two
formularies will be to this extent complementary.
On the therapeutic side, further changes have been
necessitated by the increasing use of newer and more
specific drugs together with clearer concepts as to the
pharmacological values of drug vehicles—particularly emul-
sion bases for creams and ointments. Therapeutic practice
with regard to many of these is, however, still plastic,
while the demand for a new edition of the A.P.F. is
urgent. Hence no very drastic changes may, at present,
be sanctioned.
Attention is directed to the following:—
A.P.F. Previous Editions: It is to be noted that this
Edition supersedes and replaces all previous editions of
the A.P.F. and any addenda thereto.
Prescribe/s Edition: It has always been the aim of
compilers of the A.P.F. to include only such preparations
as can he made in any reasonably equipped pharmacy and,
to ensure uniformity of the finished products, specific
directions for compounding are given in many cases. While
such directions are useful to the pharmacist, they have
little interest for the prescribes who, assured of a standard
product when he orders A.P.F. prescriptions, is concerned
only with the formulae in relation to the closes and
strengths of their active ingredients. It is therefore pro


posed to publish coincidentally with this a special Pre
scriher’s Edition to meet these requirements.
Penicillin: As a wide range of formulae for penicillin
preparations will shortly acquire official status by inclusion
in the British Pharmacopoeia, it is considered inadvisable
to include others at this stage. Actual strengths of peni-
cillin preparations must be left to the discretion of the
prescriber, but indications are given in appropriate sections
for its compounding in aqueous, emulsion or hydrophilic
bases. (See Cremores, pp. 22, 24.)
Emulsion Cream Bases: A brief classification of these
bases is given together with typical formulae in each class.
Commonly used drugs which are therapeutically and
pharmaceutically compatible are appended with each group
(See pp. 22-25.)
Prescribing Information and Dispensing Practice: This
Section has been revised and made rather more compre-
hensive. Among other things it includes definitions of
many prescribing and dispensing conventions; a classifica-
tion of emulgents in relation to the oil or water misci-
bility of the prepared emulsions; tables of isotonic and of
buffer solutions; notes on prescribing in the metric system
and conventions in relation to percentage prescriptions.
The Editorial Committee acknowledges its appreciation
of the generous contributions made by the former Editor,
Mr. H. Finnemore, b.sc. (Lon.), f.i.c., f.p.s., prior to his
retirement. Thanks are due to the Formulary Revision
Committees in the various States and New Zealand for
their helpful co-operation; to the Melbourne College of
Pharmacy for the provision of laboratory facilities; to Dr.
Adrien Albert, Ph.D., B.Sc., A.R.1.C., for his expert advice
with regard to the acridines; to Miss Miriel Witt, Ph.C.,
F.P.S., for her pharmaceutical investigations, and to many
others who have assisted in technical matters and in com-
pilation of indices, dose tables and individual sections.
Acknowledgements are due to the British Pharmaceutical
Codex, for where, in a few cases, older A.P.F. formulae
have approximated to those of the 8.P.C., these, in the
interests of uniformity have been made identical. These
formulae include Liquor Saccharini (Elixir of Gluside),
Pasta Zinci et Acicli Salicylici, and Syrupus Efypophos-
phitibus Compositus.

AMINOACRIDINA HYDROCHLORIDUM, A.P.F.
Aminoacridine Hydrochloride
Aminoacridine hydrochloride is the hydrochloride of
5-aminoacridine with one molecule of water of crystallisa-
tion: CI3HION2 • HCI. H2O . Molecular weight = 248.6.
Pale yellow, odourless, slightly hygroscopic powder with
a bitter taste. May be prepared by the action of phos-
phorus oxychloride and ammonium carbonate in turn on
phenyl-anthranilic acid.
Soluble in less than 10 parts of boiling water, and
about 300 parts of cold water.
The saturated solution is pale yellow and shows a slight
greenish-blue fluorescence, changing to an intense violet-
blue fluorescence on further dilution (e.g. to 1 in 10,000).
Soluble in about 160 parts of 90 per cent, alcohol, in
6 parts of glycerin, or in 1,000 parts of 0.45 per cent,
sodium chloride solution. Only sparingly soluble in nor-
mal saline.
The 0.1 per cent, aqueous solution has a pH of 5.5
to 6.5.
The free base, liberated with sodium hydroxide, washed
free of alkali and dried at 120 deg. C. melts at 239 to
241 deg. C. (corrected).


Aminoacridine hydrochloride may be sterilised by dry
heat at 150 deg. C. for one hour, and the aqueous solution
may be autoclaved at 120 deg. C. for thirty minutes.
Incompatible with mineral acids and with alkaline
preparations and with salts when they are present in more
than 0.5 per cent, concentration. lire solutions are stable
to light, but should be made in boiling water if they are
to be stored for any length of time, to prevent fungal
growths.
Assay: About 0.2 gramme of sample, accurately weighed,
is dissolved in 40 mils, of distilled water in a 100 mil.
standard flask. 50 mils, of N/'lO potassium dichromate,
accurately measured, is then slowly added, with constant
swirling of the flask-contents. The mixture is set aside
for 5 minutes with occasional shaking, and then adjusted
to 100 mils. The solution is filtered, the first few drops
rejected, and 50 mils, of the clear filtrate pipetted off and
diluted with distilled water to 250 mils. 5 mils concen-
trated hydrochloric acid and 1 , gramme potassium iodide
me added, and the mixture allowed to stand in the dark
for five minutes. The liberated iodine is then titrated with
N/10 sodium thiosulphate solution. Each mil. of N/10
potassium dichromate reacting with the sample is equiva-
lent to 0.00829 gramme 5-aminoacridine hydrochloride
monohydrate.
When assayed by the above procedure, Aminoacridine
Hydrochloride, A.P.F., should be found to contain 95 to
105 per cent, of 5-aminoacridine hydrochloride mono-
hydrate.

CREMORES.
Creams.
The following are introduced as types of emulsion
cream bases:—
A—Cremor Triethanolaminae Stearatis, A.P.F.
B—Cremor Glycerylis Monostearatis, A.P.F.
C—Cremor Cerae Sulphonatae, A.P.F.
These three creams are oil-in-water bases which make
intimate contact with the skin, and are suitable for trans-
mitting ingredients, e.g., penicillin, sulphonamides, etc.,
and can be removed without friction by gentle washing
with water.
Because of their efficiency in delivering medicaments
to the skin caution must be used with regard to the
strengths of more potent drugs prescribed with them, e.g.,
phenols, mercurials, etc.
D—Cremor Plydrophilicum, A.P.F.
This is a hydrophilic or potential oil-in-water base,
miscible with moist surfaces to make an emulsion through
which medicaments are conveyed.
E—Cremor Oleosum, A.P.F. '
F—Cremor Aquosum, A.P.F.
These are water-in-oil bases, which, while making in-
timate contact with the skin, are protective and emollient
rather than transmitters of drugs.

HOSPITAL SECTION
of the
AUSTRALIAN AND NEW ZEALAND
PHARMACEUTICAL FORMULARY
1947
For
HOSPITAL & CONTRACT
DISPENSING
This section comprises a range of formulae sufficiently
wide to meet most general prescribing requirements.


In order that any formula in this Hospital Section may
be adjusted to individual requirements with regards to
the dosage of its basic ingredients, the following con-
vention should he observed:—
Mist. Acid. Acetylsal. A.P.F., as an example of a simple
prescription, contains 10 grains of Acetylsalicylic Acid in
the half-ounce dose. If a larger dose of the basic drug be
regarded as essential, a prescription written “Mist. Acid.
Acetylsalicyl. A.P.F. 15 grains” will be dispensed with
15 grains of Acetylsalicylic Acid to the half-ounce dose, the
other ingredients remaining unchanged.
When a smaller quantity of the basic drug is ordered—
e.g., “Mist. Acid. Acetylsalicyl. A.P.F., 5 gr.,” the standard
mixture will be dispensed, the dose being reduced to that
containing the specified quantity of drug—in this case 120
minims. For convenience in administration, odd quantities
of the standard mixture containing the specified dose will
be made up to 60, 120, or 240 minims.
Such an arrangement will allow of many of the pre-
scriptions in the general section being modified for
children. This, in fact, has been done in some of the
formulae in the Children’s Section and, as the formulae
in such cases bear the same names as the corresponding
ones in the general section, care must be exercised in
prescribing from this section to add to the title the quali-
fying suffix “pro infantibus.”
The same convention with regard to the modification
of doses applies also to compound prescriptions and, to
facilitate its application, the names of the basic and
adjuvant drugs are included in the title. The doses of
either or both may thus be suitably altered—e.g., “Mist.
Chloral. (12 gr.) et Bromid. (10 gr.) A.P.F.”

PRESCRIBING INFORMA-
TION AND DISPENSING
PRACTICE
1. All prescriptions should be written in ink and the
signature of the Prescriber written in full. A rubber stamp
may not be used in place of a written signature.
2. When it is necessary to prescribe an unusually large
dose, i.e., one in excess of that of the B.P. or 8.P.C., the
quantity should be underlined and initialled by the Pre-
scriber; any alteration in a prescription should be initialled.
3. When an unusually large dose appears to have been
prescribed, it shall be the duty of the Pharmacist to satisfy
himself that the Prescriber’s intentions have been correctly
interpreted. In cases where it is impossible to communi-
cate with the Prescriber, and in the absence of such
underlining and initialling of an unusually large dose, the
Pharmacist must reduce the dose to one he considers is
within the limits of safety. He should indicate in a
marginal note on the prescription the action taken, and
initial the same.
4. In cases where there is any justifiable doubt as to
the interpretation of a prescription, it is the duty of the
Pharmacist, before dispensing the same, to communicate
with the Prescriber if possible. Such communication
should be made privately.
5 . When substances are prescribed which are physically
or chemically incompatible, the Pharmacist must ensure:—
(a) that the patient obtains the ingredients of the pre-
scription in accurately divided doses, and
(b) that no substance of a deleterious nature is formed
by mixing these ingredients.
In such cases the Pharmacist may make simple additions
to the vehicle of a prescription without consultation with
the Prescriber, e.g., the addition of a necessary suspending
agent, or a slight alteration in the basis of an ointment,
provided that the nature of the vehicle is not appreciably
altered. Marginal notes of such additions or alterations
must be made on the prescription, and the Prescriber
informed if the importance of the alteration justifies such
action.


6. In all cases where the Pharmacist is compelled to
communicate with the Prescriber, the latter will recognise
that the Pharmacist is solely concerned in performing a
necessary part of his professional duty by co-operation in
the interests of the patient.
7. Before communicating with the Prescriber the
Pharmacist will attempt to deal with any difficulty to the
best of his ability and should be able to suggest to the
Prescriber a practicable method of overcoming such
difficulty.
8. Because of the status of the British Pharmacopoeia
as a legal standard, it is obligatory on the Pharmacist to
dispense the official product whenever a drug is prescribed
by its pharmacopoeial name or by any of the official
synonyms.
THYROIDEUM, B.P.
Synonyms: Thyroideum Siccum; Dry Thyroid; Thyroid
Extract; Thyroid Gland.
Difficulties have occurred with thyroid gland in view
of the persistence of an older practice of prescribing it in
terms of the corresponding weight of fresh gland, of which
five grains are equivalent to about one and a half grains
(Wi grains) of the pharmacopoeial product.
When prescribed without qualification as Thyroideum
or as any of the above synonyms, the official product,
standardised to contain 0.1 per cent, iodine combined as
thyroxine, must be dispensed.
Should any non-pharmacopoeial preparation be required
it is necessary to specify this in exact terms in the pre-
scription to avoid any possible error.
Non-Official Formulae.— lf a preparation is ordered with-
out specifying a formula, such preparation of the A.P.F.
with a corresponding title will be dispensed.
Weights and Measures.— Liquids are dispensed by
volume (irrespective of their specific gravity), solids by
weight.
Whenever the sign is used in prescriptions, it is in-
variably understood to indicate one fluid ounce of a liquid
or 480 grains of a solid. Without further specification,
1 oz. signifies one fluid ounce for a liquid, and 437.5
grains for a solid.
Percentage Prescriptions in both the Metric and the
Imperial Systems are made up to 100 parts by volume if


liquid, regardless of specific gravity, or if solid or semi-
solid, to 100 parts by weight. The constituents of such
prescriptions are dispensed by volume if liquid, or by
weight if they are solid. They are defined as weight in
volume, w/v; weight in weight, w/w; volume in volume,
v/v.
Thus:—
A 1% solution of a solid in liquid w/v contains 4.375
grains of solid per fluid ounce (or 1 grain in 110 minims).
A 1% solution of a solid or liquid in solid w/w contains
4.8 grains of solid or liquid in 480 grains (or 1 grain of
solid in 100 grains; or 1.1 minims of liquid in 100
grains).
A 1% solution of liquid in liquid v/v contains 4.8
minims of liquid in 1 fl. ounce of solution (or 1 minim
in 100 minims).

Emulsification.
1. General Method.— Use 4 of Oil in 1 of Acacia in
powder. Mix the Acacia in powder in a mortar with the
whole of the oil. Add all at once using a non-oily
measure, 2of water. Stir lightly until a “primary emul-
sion” results, then dilute gradually with water and add
the other ingredients.
2. Minimal Gum Method.—Where the product contains
a high percentage of Oil, use 8 of oil to 1 of Acacia in
powder. Prepare a primary emulsion as described above,
again using 4 of Oil to 1 of Acacia. Now gradually in-
corporate the rest of the oil alternately with water. When
all the oil is incorporated dilute with water to make the
required volume.
3. Mucilage of Acacia Method.—Use 8 of Oil to 1 of
Mucilage of Acacia. To the fresh mucilage in a mortar
add the oil very gradually, emulsifying between each
addition. Dilute and add the other ingredients. This
method has the advantage that the mucilage may be
sterilised, or deprived of its oxidising ferments by heat, and
strained before use. If powders are present they should be
stirred up with the oil before adding it to the emulgent.
Note.—' The proportions of oil, water and gum above
refer to the emulsification of fixed oils. For volatile oils
use twice the proportion of gum as for fixed oils.


Stabilization.—All the above emulsions may be stabilized
by the addition of Mucilage of Tragacanth or Decoction
of Irish Moss.

Suppositories.
Suppositories made with Cocoa Butter base usually
weigh 15 grains each. Allowance must be made in calcu-
lating quantities required, for what is termed the displace-
ment value of the medicament. When the medicament
occupies approximately the same volume as an equal
weight of Cocoa Butter it is said to have a displacement
value of 1. To prepare a suppository containing such a
medicament, omit one grain of Cocoa Butter for each
grain of medicament. If the material occupies half the

BUFFERS AND BUFFER SOLUTIONS.
The hydrogen-ion concentration of solutions of many
substances may require adjusting and buffering to a
definite pH value in order that it will remain unchanged
on the addition of small quantities of acid or alkali, and
so the stability of the solution will be maintained.
The pH of a solution may be adjusted by adding solu-
tions of acid or alkali, e.g., N/10 HCI or N/10 NaOH,
drop by drop, and determining the pH value of the solu-
tion after each addition until the required pH is attained.
The pH may be determined electrometrically or colori-
metrically. When the colorimetric method is used, solu-
tions of standard pH (B.P. 1932, p. 525), may be used for
purposes of comparison.
To buffer a solution mixtures are usually employed,
consisting of:
(a) a slightly ionised acid and its neutral sodium or
potassium salt;
(b) a slightly ionised base and one of its strongly ionised
salts, e.g. chloride.
Buffers in common use are:—
1. Boric acid and sodium borate.
2. Acetic acid and sodium acetate.
3. Citric acid and sodium citrate.
4. Sodium Acid Phosphate B.P. (NaH2PO4) and
Sodium Phosphate B.P. (NaaHPOA.
5. Ammonium hydroxide and ammonium chloride.

GENERAL PRINCIPLES OF TREATMENT.
(1) Remove poisons from stomach by emetics—and/or
stomach tube.
(2) Administer specific antidotes, which may be—
(a) mechanical
(b) chemical
(c) therapeutic.
NOTE.—(I) and (2) will be carried out together by
using specific antidote with or preceding emetic or
by gastric lavage through tube with specific antidote.
(3) Treat secondary symptoms. Shock and collapse,
threatened cardiac or respiratory failure, pain, thirst,
etc.
(4) Eliminate by purgation, diuresis and diaphoresis,
saline transfusion, enemata, etc.

THE REMOVAL OF STAINS
When stains or dyes come into contact with the skin,
articles of clothing or household linen, they may, as a
rule, be removed by immediately washing with cold water.
If, however, the stain is allowed to dry, or is exposed to the
action of light, or of hot water, or if attempts be made to
remove it by the use of soap, soda, ammonia, borax, wash-
ing powders or other alkalies, chemical union may take
place between the stain and the fabric, with the result that
the two can only be separated by the use of chemical
bleaching or decolorising agents. Although chemicals may
remove the stain they nearly all produce some weakening
of the fabric; those recommended in the following notes
have been chosen as the least destructive. Coloured
materials may also undergo some bleaching, because it
is almost impossible to employ a bleaching agent which
will select the stain and not affect the colour of the
article. This destructive effect may not be apparent until
the article is subsequently used and washed. Some arti-

Warning: It should therefore be made quite clear, in
writing if necessary, that no responsibility is taken as to
any damage to the article. Stains may be treated with
the following:—
1. Cold Water. 2. Fat or Grease Solvents.
3. Bleaching Agents.
1. COLD WATER TREATMENT.
A stream of water, in the opposite direction to which
the dye was applied, should always be tried, or an immedi-
ate rinsing with cold water. Small areas may be sponged
with a small pad moistened with water. Water should
not he applied if the stain is known or suspected to be
grease or fat.
2. GREASE, FAT OR OIL SOLVENTS.
Grease, fat or oil stains should not be wetted with
water, but may be treated with any inexpensive solvent
of fats, such as petroleum ether (also called benzine);
benzene, or the less expensive mixture of benzene and
toluene (known as benzole); petrol, either alone or mixed


with an equal volume of carbon tetrachloride to render it
non-inflammable; chloroform; acetone; ether; methylated
spirit. Caution is necessary in the use of all these sol-
vents; some are inflammable and should therefore never
be used in a room in which there is either a fire, gas
stove, or an electric heater. Others again are or become
poisonous when they are vaporized and should not be
inhaled. Turpentine, not the “turps” of the oil shops, and
oil of eucalyptus may sometimes be used. Grease solvents
may be used in several ways, (a) The solvent may be
placed in a shallow wide-mouthed bottle and the stained
material screwed into a plug and inserted. The solvent
will show the progress that is being made. In some cases
the pure solvent has little action and it is an advantage
to smear olive or arachis oil or oleic acid on the stain
before the application of the solvent. (b} The stain
may be sponged with a pad moistened with the solvent,
taking care to work from the outside to the centre of
the stain, (c) A ring of solvent is first made outside of
the stain which is then covered with clean blotting
paper or rag; the solvent is now applied to the stain
and the paper or rag is pressed upon it. ( d ) A warm
iron may be passed over the oily stain previously enclosed
between two layers of clean, absorbent brown or blotting
paper.
3. REMOVAL BY BLEACHING AGENTS.
In using bleaching agents for the removal of stains
consideration must always be given to the danger of
destroying the fabric. As a rule wool and silk should
not be exposed to the action of the alkaline substances
mentioned in the first paragraph of these notes. Cotton
and linen are not affected by alkalies, hut all fabrics
are destroyed by acids. Even vegetable acids are slightly
destructive, and weak mineral acids become concentrated
and highly destructive on exposure. It is therefore a wise
precaution, after the use of chemicals to deacidify, so as
to ensure that all acids have been removed. This is
done by rinsing the article in water to which some
whiting or chalk has been added.
(a)SODIUM HYDROSULPHITE This
should be distinguished from so-called sodium hypo-
sulphite (thiosulphate). It is a white powder which may


he either sprinkled on the moistened stain, or it may be
dissolved in water and the stained fabric dipped in.
As some sulphurous acid is always produced during its
bleaching action, and this may oxidise to sulphuric acid
if not removed, the treatment with chalk mentioned
on page 173 should not be omitted.
(b) PERMANGANATE AND SULPHITE. For a
small stain apply one or two drops of a 1% solution of
potassium permanganate, then apply a 5% solution of
either sulphurous acid alone, or sodium sulphite, bisul-
phite or metabisulphite, acidified with a little acid, such
as acetic, or vinegar, or with lemon juice. One, two or
three short treatments may be required. In general it is
better to give short treatments rather than a prolonged one.
For larger stains the article may be wrung in the solution
of permanganate and then immersed in the sulphite.
Deacidifv with whiting or chalk as above.
(c) HYPOCHLORITES, such as the calcium or
sodium salts have only rarely been recommended in these
notes. They are sometimes very effective and may be
used, but only when other means fail, because of their
destructive action upon the material. The solution of
sodium hypochlorite of the Pharmacopoeia, or a little
bleaching powder enclosed in a small piece of cloth should
be gently rubbed on the stain previously moistened with
water or dilute acid, vinegar or lemon juice. It is highly
important to remove the last trace of hypochlorite after-
wards, and this may be done by immersion in a weak
solution of sodium thiosulphate.
(d) HYDROGEN PEROXIDE may be used in some
cases. A few drops of ammonia are added to the peroxide
(diluted with 2—5 times its volume of water), then a few
drops of 1% solution of copper sulphate as a catalyst.
