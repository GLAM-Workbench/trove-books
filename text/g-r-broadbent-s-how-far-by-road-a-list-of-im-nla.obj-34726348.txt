Directory of Towns
and their distances from Melbourne.
CH E subjoined list of towns with their respective dis-
tances from the Metropolis has been compiled in res-
ponse to, and in compliance with, many requests from
tourists and other classes of travellers who desired to
ascertain with the* least possible delay the length of the
journey to a given place, without the trouble of adding
together 1 the mileage between the intermediate towns as
indicated on the map. The distances are given in round
numbers, all fractions of a mile being omitted; the best —not
necessarily the shortest —route has been adopted, and al-
though the distances are generally approximate, they will be
found to be near enough for all practical purposes.
The cities and principal towns only are included, but the
distance to any village not tabulated in this list may be
obtained by finding that to the most important town nearest
to it, and counting up the intermediate mileage on the map.,

“ Fingerposts, with the Towns and dis-
tances marked upon them, and Mile-posts
are to the Country what an Index and
pages are to a book; They enable one to
reach his objective in the shortest possible
time and with the minimum of trouble.”
Who is it, at some time when travelling, has not gone
astray at some puzzling road-junction, for the want of a
helping hand ? In the metropolitan area, if anyone is
searching for a street, and finds that the name of any
thoroughfare is not displayed, he becomes righteously in-
dignant. But, with people passing every minute, and
with houses on every side, his condition is not to be com-
pared with that of the triveller on the country roads, who-
at a point where several tracks diverge, may wait an hour
m vain for someone to pass, or scan the land for a habita-
tion without satisfactory result. If it be necessary, or even
desirable that the streets in the city and suburbs be
placarded, how much more so , should all junctions and
crossroads be furnished with road signs indicating whence
e branches run and the distances thereto, respectively,
t would appear that the average local council has become
remiss during the last generation, for at one time the shires
caused both finger-boards and mile-posts to be erected,
some of which stand to-day, although much dilapidated-
indeed, in many instances the posts only remain; yet the
councillors are unmoved, though they may pass them
ft 1 y "u • “ ere . S-tGi however, some exceptions. Several of
, i r j-’ wUh men of the new generation in office, have
erected direction-boards at junctions, and, in a few cases,
renovated the weather-stained mile-posts. Yet some of
these, too, m the matter of the finger-posts, have only gone
half way. They have omitted to indicate the distance to
the various towns mentioned.
The general neglect of erecting suitable road signs at
even the most important junctions has done much to dis-
courage tourmg and travel generally. The more the people
e-an be induced to move about the land the better for the
country generally. It is circulation; it is life, no matter
ow much councillors may deprecate what
they are pleased to term “foreign traffic.” As a matter
o fact, if there were no such foreign traffic for them, they
wn d soon, become useless; they could not live alone.
When (if ever) the erection of finger-boards and mile-
posts again becomes general, the Government must afford
r lem some measure of protection (as is accorded telegraph
poles, etc.), to guard them from the vagaries of quasi-
sportsmen, who, lacking better game, too frequently riddle
foe signs with small shot.
[The foregoing appeared iu an early edition of Broadbent’s
Road and Railway Map of Victoria. It is more applicable to-
day than ever before].
