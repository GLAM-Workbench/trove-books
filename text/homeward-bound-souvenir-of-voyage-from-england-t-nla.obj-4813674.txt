FAREWELL MESSAGE.
I think I can safely say that our long sea voyage
from Liverpool was quite a happy one, and I am certain
all of you are as glad as I to be once more in your home-
land after the weary years of war. I take this oppor-
tunity of bidding you all "au-revoir," and of wishing you
the best of health and success in your future careers.
We have all learned lessons during the war. One
that is impressed very much on our memories is the
lesson that we belong to a country of unlimited possi-
bilities—a country with an immense future if we only
are united in our efforts as citizens of such a grand
Commonwealth; so it is up to us all to face the problems
of our return to civil life with that tenacity and courage
that won so much fame for us on foreign battlefields.
I felt it an honour to command the men who
returned on the "Argyllshire," and the remembrance of
the voyage will always be a happy one.
I feel I should mention the names of my Adjutant
Lieut. W. Swinton, A.F.C.—whose untiring energy con-
tributed so largely to the success of the voyage; the
Ship's Sergt.-Major —B.S.M. Bowditch (M.S.M.) ; Sergt,
McGarvie (Orderly Room Sergt.), and Cpl. Pollock, of
A.M.C. details, for their devotion to duty during the
voyage.
With all good wishes,
I am, yours, etc.,
J. P. CLARK, Lt.-Col.,
44th Batt.


HOMEWARD BOUND.
ABOUT OUR SHIP.
The "Argyllshire" was built at Clydebank by Messrs. John
Brown & Co., and has a tonnage of 12,500. Her length is 550 feet,
and breadth 61 feet 6 ins. She is commanded by Capt. W. J. Page—
a well-known Federal Shire Line Skipper—and if he were not so
modest, we would have been able to learn a lot more about the
narrow escapes this vessel has had during the war.
The "Argyllshire" was engaged during hostilities in carrying
troops from Australia, and from the United Kingdom to Salonika.
She was in the original convoy from Australia. Captain Page, in
an interview, kindly consented to tell a little about one of the
narrow escapes he had from disaster. After inviting our repre-
sentative to "have another"—look out the porthole, he said:—
"The vessel was proceeding without escort from London to
Devonport for the purpose of taking aboard wounded Australian
soldiers. When but a short distance off Start Point, the steamer
was struck by . a torpedo, which exploded aft, completely wrecking
the stern post, rudder, and other steering gear. A fortunate and
very curious feature of the explosion was that while practically
the whole of the steamer's stern was blown away, the two screws
remained in place on the starboard and port side of the wrecked
stern, to which the rudder post and rudder had been attached.
The ship commenced to fill rapidly, and for some moments it
appeared that the crew would have to take to the boats. The
main watertight bulkheads held, and when the electrical pumps
were operated at full pressure it was decided that, although the
water was gaining on the pumps, there was a chance of getting
the vessel to Plymouth, about 30 miles distant. The engineroom
bulkheads were severely strained, and the engineers were obliged
to attend to their duties with the sea water swirling around them.
"With all the steering gear destroyed and the steamer well
down at the stern, navigation by the port and starboard screws,
based on telegraphed orders from the bridge, was a hazardous
undertaking, the successful performance of which depended largely
on the skipper on the bridge and the chief engineer in the engine-
room. When examination had shown that the engines were not
damaged, a zig-zag course was set comparatively close in to the
coast. Wireless messages were despatched requesting tug-boat
assistance. On two or three occasions, as the stricken steamer
limped towards port, it appeared that the vessel would have to
be beached without delay, but each time, the chief engineer, after
consulation with the skipper, decided to 'give her another five
minutes. When about 10 miles off Plymouth, tug-boats came in
sight, but as the steamer was steaming faster than the tugs the
Captain decided to decline immediate assistance and continued
the steering with the propellors. Plymouth Sound was reached

A THOUGHT ON RETURNING HOME.
By the time our "Souvenir Book" is in the hanas of its readers,
most of those who were fellow-passengers on the "Argyllshire" will
have settled down once more in their native towns. The festivities
of the welcome home will be ended; the round of rejoicing and
pleasure will be drawing to a close, and we shall each of us be
rapidly approaching the day when we shall have again to face
the stern duties of civil life. During the voyage we have tried,
to some extent, to prepare ourselves for the renewal of our rights
of citizenship. In the debates which we have had together our
thoughts have been drawn from the memories of battlefield, camp
and the old Homeland to important political and social matters
upon which our own country will require our opinion and vote.
In the lectures, to which we here listened, we have had suggested
to us many matters, which we may well study further, to our own
and our neighbour's benefit, and some have made more definite
preparation in the classes on various subjects which they have
attended.
After many months, and in most cases years, of the turmoil
of war, it is not going to be a matter of easy accomplishment
to throw off the martial spirit and to take up once more the
simple life of peace, but far harder is it going to be to learn
that that simple life of peace demands from us as great a display
of patriotism, as keen a devotion to duty, and as stern self-discipline
as the hard campaign through which we have so successfully
passed.
The great lesson, which the war has brought before the minds
of all the peoples of the world, is undoubtedly the simple fact that
the nation which forgets the elementary law of righteousness, and
fails to remember that the possession of might and power does
not justify a disregard of the just claims of the weaker ones of
the earth, is bound, sooner or later, to come to destruction. The
overthrow of our enemies has surely taught us also this lesson,
that the only form of social life which is worth having, and which
will be secure from ultimate destruction is that which is founded
on righteousness —righteousness in negotiations between nation and
nation —righteousness in all dealings between employer and em-
ployee—righteousness in our relations between man and man. But
"God is righteousness," says the Book which we all revere. The
people then, which will last, and whose name shall be blessed
through all the ages to come, and shall never see destruction
by its enemies, is the people whose watchword is "Righteousness,"
that is, "God."
Every man who enlisted was prepared if necessary to "die"
for Australia; now let us go out to "live" for Australia by prac-
tising daily the great lesson which the downfall of our enemies
has taught us—that by righteousness alone shall a people be saved
from destruction. SIDNEY A. BEVERIDGE,
Chaplain C. of E.

WAR EXPERIENCES.
Those who do not know me think I am a rainbow. That is
only natural. All the books one gets at the Y.M. library agree
that modesty becomes a hero. I do not wear my A.'s or other
decorations fpr the reason. However, I'm getting away from my
subject. I only :hope, Mr. Editor, that you will respect my feelings
and not let a whisper of my identity float outside the Editorial
Sanctum. I become very confused when, as often happens, praise
descends on me like an eclipse of the sun for one or other of my
humble efforts during the din and strife of battle. I shall never
forget so long as I live my confusion and blushes when I had to
appear before His Most Gracious Majesty King George for—but
again I wander. Before dealing with my experiences proper I
may state I am not anxious to obtain a prize for this account of
my humble efforts to help quell Armaggedon. There will be other
entrants who need the money more than I do, though the articles
they submit—again I wander. In support of this I must say I
am well provided for in civil life. I insured my mother-in-law
for two thousand prior to setting out on the great adventure.
Since then the unfortunate woman, in a moment of madness, tried
to stop an electric car with her back. I also drew ten shillings on
the boat, and by judicious investment in "Housey," I feel sure
of visiting our various ports of call with a jingle in the pocket.
I have also had good luck with my issue of comfort fags; they
were only mouldy at one end, so I light the end that is not mouldy.
Luck, as you will read, plays a great part in my experiences. It
was just sheer good luck that allotted me to the A.S.C.; some said
I had influence, but on my word of honour I deny it. I hope my
comrades will not think I am a braggart when they have finished
reading this; it ill becomes a true digger to boast of his exploits,
but I really am not capable of writing personal experiences unless
I make mention of myself. Though I write like a gentleman I
wish to state I am not an officer. A commission never appealed
to me, but I often wished I had been a batman. After our first
big engagement my. Colonel, amongst other distinctions, recom-
mended me for a commission. As this would have necessitated my
evacuating my post on the cheese dump, which I held with much
profit and satisfaction to all concerned, I had to firmly decline.
I preferred to remain a private on congenial work rather than live
a life of idleness as a lone star artist. My work was not, how-
ever, to remain unrewarded, and after the Armistice I was promoted
to the rank of Lance-Corporal. Here I must again mention another
instance of the good luck which never deserted me in the thickest
of the strife. There were three of us all practically of the same
merit, aspirants for the coveted stripe. There was much heart
burning as to on whom the coveted honour was to descend. For-
tunately my two opponents, having filled up one evening on Vin
Blanc, tried conclusions with the Jacks —result, disastrous to them—-

It would not be right for me to quote literally from my diary

have passed through the gates of hell have no desire to dwell
upon our experiences therein. Maybe amongst those who read
will be some of those gallant young diggers in the making, who
took over and tenderly treated our donks, O.C.'s and other gear
necessary for the carrying on of a great war, but who, alas, were
held back by bonds, we know not of, until our enemy striveth no
more. It were not right that their fancies should be of morbid
things, bloody sights, bellies that acheth for the bully beef we
combeeanged, rather let their thoughts be turned to the honour
and glory awaiting us all on Austral shores. I hold with the best
authors and our educational officer that the aim of true literature
is to educate and amuse. So far I have only dealt with experiences
when the dump was situated in No Man's Land. Owing to the
daring and frequency of the wily Huns' raids on the Stilton, the
G.O.C. gave orders to retire to the support trenches. Our new
posey in the supports was flanked on one side by Dados, and on
the other by the Motor Transport repair workshops. Between
us and the Hun trenches was a matter of about eight hundred
yards, but with our gallant infantry in front, and the strong and
valiant troops we had on either flank, I may say, to use the ex-
pressive words of the language of our brave Allies, it was tres
bon. New discoveries in the art of camouflage enabled us to
successfully disguise the smell emanating from the Stilton dump,
so that its whereabouts remained a mystery to the nightly parties
of raiding Huns. Just about this time the famous division to
which I belong was ordered to CHARGE every man minus his
iron ration the sum of three shillings, so as can be imagined life
on the dump became a constant period of watchfulness. I cannot
here refrain from giving a boquet of praise to the gallant red
tabs of ours, the valiant "Eggs a cooked" divvy, or as our comrades
of the other divvies, in more flowery language, pay us the tribute
when they call us the "Swans." There on the dump it was we saw
and admired the self-sacrificing work of our oftimes maligned
red tabs. Since my promotion to non-commissioned rank, I have
read in King's Regs., para. 98, that a soldier must not praise his
superiors per medium of paper, so perforce I must refrain from
giving full praise until such time as my well-worn Bruce takes the
place of the uniform I have so long done honour to. Modesty
compels me to say that my experiences on Stilton dump were no
harder than that of many other of our brave fellows, alas no
more. I must say that at times my responsibility was greater owing
to the habit many of our Stiltons had of evacuating the position
and going A.W.L. You diggers who had at times the dubious plea-
entries on their conduct sheets removed all opposition, and my

IMAGINATIONS.
(By 5.8.)
Clearly something was afoot. Beneath the hustle of City life
one could detect the furtive air of expectancy. The further one
went the more evident it became. Outside the several banks,
gangs of men were at work erecting door and window barriers.
Queues of brewers' lorries stood outside the hotels discharging
their loads. Most every restaurant had provided an extra door,
and in addition had engaged extra staffs. Victoria Barracks now
resembled an "Information Bureau," for each of the 249 windows
were respectively labelled "enquiries," whilst the hotel opposite
had bought up all the available adjoining property and enlarged
its premises. Throughout the night, the Lord Mayor sat locked
in his room committing the Riot Act to memory, whilst it was
freely rumoured that the "John 'Ops" had been issued with field
dressings. Mid-day had barely chimed forth from the G.P.O. when
a street scavenger, pausing- in his duties, gave vent to a yell, "She's
'ere." An astonished pedestrian looked up from his "Sportsman"
and enquired, "Who's here?" A mingled look of disgust and in-
credulity spread over the scavenger's face. Dexterously expec-
torating over his left shoulder, he exclaimed: "Ain't you ever 'eard
of the 'Argyllshire'?"

MILESTONES.
The days are often dreary,
And the skies are sometimes black;
And the sun-god doesn't always
Shed his beams around our track;
Our hearts are sad and restless
As afar our footsteps roam,
But it's lovely counting milestones
On the journey—going home.
When we know not where we're going,
Or to where the path may lead;
The mole-hills seem like mountains,
Small cares grow great indeed;
But we do not heed the mountain,
The desert or the foam,
If we know we're passing milestones
On the journey—going home.
There we know the friends we cherish,
And our loved ones will at last,
Bid us welcome to the homeland
When the milestones all are passed,
So comrades leave your wanderings,
And leave your cares and come,
And prove it's lovely counting
The milestones—going home.

NO MANS LAND.
(A strip of land between the hostile trenches.)
(By the late Lt. H. R. Bruford.)
No ghastlier land was ever spread
Beneath the dome of Northern skies,
Than that where now the sleeping dead
Turn heavenward their sightless eyes.
The bullets pass like sobs of pain,
Or whimpers from a child who knows
The meaning of the crimson stain
That on a bleeding finger grows.
And naked claws of tangled wire
Lie hidden deeply in the grass,
Where coil and coil, with points of fire,
Reach forth to rend who try to pass.
With screams of woe the shrapnel sheds
A rain of steel to crush and maim—
A vagrant comet torn to shreds—
A star that burst in wreaths of flame.
And with the dark some devil spills
A bowl of poison fell and green;
Cloud follows cloud and vapour fills
The weeping landscape with chlorine.
No human horde that ever trod
Its savage pathway through the world,
No cataclysm sent from God
Such bulk of dire destruction hurled.
But ev'n from farthest lands there come,
The grim avengers of a lust,
In realm despoiled and ravished home,
That tramples freedom in the dust.
Battalions thirsting with desire
To venture life, to cross the strand
Where death stalks garmented in fire,
By flail of myriad bullets fanned.
So when the day—The Day—is ripe,
God grant that I may live to stand
And watch the gathered legions wipe
The curse of hell from "No Man's Land."

FROM WAR TO PEACE.
(By the Editor.)
And so we are once more in our home-towns after having
taken our part in the greatest of world dramas. We can hold up
our heads with pride to think we were with the gallant actors
from Australia—for our Corps has done mighty things towards
helping to free the world from the menace of Prussian domination.
Many of you are in the, heart of your beloved bush; many are
beside the peaceful Pacific Ocean. They are fortunate men who
have returned with health and strength after the memorable tragedy
just recently closed. But it will take a long time to forget the
horrors of Gallipoli, the mud of Northern France and Flanders,
and the names of our pals who crossed the borderland. For years
we marched and fought, and lived beside the sound of guns—but
stuck the horrors of the vast abomination. Everything that can
be written about the horror of war—should be written; some-
thing real and vital that would strip it of any impressions
of glamour, so that those coming after might strive to
prevent such national calamities. It's nothing but a loathsome
murder-job—a degrading monstrosity—in which men toil out their
hearts and choke out their souls. We ought to be living in an age
when such torments should be unknown—and now that the wild
beast of Prussia has been slaughtered—an era of peace and pro-
gress is within our grasp.
Many of you, even amidst your memories of war in France,
will have a thought for the long spring days away from the line,
the miles of buttercups, cornflowers, and poppies in the grass, and
the sweet-scented hawthorn along the hedges. You will also re-
member your visits to the wondrous cathedrals of Amiens and
Rouen, and other places; your visit to the dreamland capital (Paris),
and your breakfasts, luncheons, and dinners of bully-beef. Truly it
was a wonderful experience—and I'm certain each one of you feels
a better man for having joined in the fight.
I trust this little booklet will bring you a happy reminder
of your journey home. Any of the discomforts of the troopship
were very much overbalanced by the fact that each day brought
you closer to this sun-kissed land of your "Home, Sweet, Home."
