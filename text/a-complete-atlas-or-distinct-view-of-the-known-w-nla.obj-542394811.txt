Particular Maps being Parts of this, retain the

lar Part 5 which are made fmaller or larger, as the
Paper on which it is drawn, will admit; and the
Diftance of Places mentioned in it are always pro-

let a Map be ever fo fmall, the Diftance of Places

The principal Ufe of Maps is to difeover the

Latitude and Longitude.
Latitude is theDiftanceof any Place from each
Side of the Equator of the Poles • which
Diftance being but ninety Degrees each, no La-
titude can exceed that Number.
Longitude is the Diftance of a Place from the
firft Meridian. This may be any Meridian taken
at pleafure j but in thefe Maps is that which pafleth
thro’ London , the Metropolis of our own Nation,
and is divided into 360 Parts or Degrees. Longi-
tude is two-fold, either Eaft or Weft, as the Reckon-
ing is made to the Eaft orWeft of the fir
In all Maps the North is at Top, the South at
Bottom, the on the Right, and the Weft on
the Left 5 or if it be otherwife, it is always ex-
prefled, either by Words on each Side, or by a
Mariner’s Compafs, wherein the Mark of a Flower-
de-Luce always denotes the North.
The Degrees of Latitude are always marked on
the Eaft and Weft Side of the Map. Thofe of
Longitude on the North and South.
A Degree of Latitude is always of the fame
Breadth: Wherefore the Diftance of two Places

known by knowing the different Latitude .
But a Degree of Longitude is of different Extent#
and varies in every new Parallel of Latitude 5 for
all the Meridian-lines meeting and interfering each
other at the Poles, the Degrees of Longitude do
naturally diminifti as they proceed either way from
the Equaton The beft Explication of which is
an Orange with the Peel ftript off, where the na-

the Meridians of a Globe eroding each other at
the Top and Bottom : Whereas if the Orange be
cut in dices the contrary way, without removing
them out of their Places, the Dividons are parallel,
and the Degrees of Latitude all equal.
The Longitude of any Place [from London ] be-
ing known, the Difference in the Hour of the
Day is alfo known. For as the Sun performs his
diurnal Circuit in 24 Hours, he gains in each Hour
15 Degrees, being a 24 th Part of 360, or one De-
gree in four Minutes. So that at any Place 1 c
Degrees Eaft of us, Noon is an Hour fooner with
them, as it is an Hour later with thofe who live
15 Degrees Weft of us. The Town of Pembroke
in Wales being five Degrees Weft of London) their
Noon is therefore 20 Minutes later.
The Latitude and Longitude of a Place being
known, you may find it immediately in the Map
by drawing a Line or Thread crofs the Map both
ways, and where the two Lines cut one another,
the Place ftands.
The general Divifion of the Globe is into Land
and Water.
The Land is fubdivided into feveral Parts, whofe
Names are as follow :
A Continent is a large Space of dry Land com-
prehending many Countries all joined together,
without any Separation by Water. Thus Europe ,
Afia, and Africa is one great Continent, as Ame-
rica is another.
An JJland is a Part of dry Land encompaffed

is one Ifland, and Irela?id another.
An Archipelago is a Clufter of fmall Iflands

A Peninfula is a Part of dry Land encom-*
palled with Water, except on one narrow Side,
which joins it to the Land, as the Morea in
Greece.
An IJlhmus is a narrow Neck of Land, which
joins a Peninfula to the Continent, as Darien and
Corinth .

Some Parts of the Ocean run up into the Land

the Baltic Sea , the Red Sea , &c. the latter are
called
Gulphs , as the Gulph of Venice , the Gulphs of
Bothnia and Finland , &c.
But Beds of Water are called Seas , altho’ they
lie between Lands, and communicate with other
Seas thro’ a narrow Strait, if they are very large,
as the Euxine Sea , the Red Sea •, and fometimes,
though they have no Communication, as the
Cafpian Sea.
A Strait is a narrow Arm of the Sea having
Land on both Sides. A Strait is either between
one great Sea and another, between the Ocean
and a Gulph, or between one Gulph and another.
As for Example, 1. The Strait of Magellan lies
between the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans. 2. The
Strait of Gibraltar, thro’ which the Atlantic Ocean
flows into the Mediterranean Sea. Or, 3. The
Hellefpont and the Strait of Confiantinople ; the

A Bay is a Part of the Sea, half furrounded by
the Land, or at leafl bounded by two Promonto-
ries 5 as the Bay of Bifcay , the Bay of Bengal,
and the Bay of Mexico.
A Harbour is generally the Bottom of a Bay,
or the Mouth of a large River, fo called as being
a convenient Place for Shipping to lie fecure from
Storms. We call the Bottom of a Bay the inner-
moft Part of it towards Land.
A Road is a Place in the Sea, a little off Land,
where there is fome Shelter and good Anchorage
for Ships to ride.
A Channel is an Arm of the Sea running be-
tween two Shores, wider than what we call a Strait;
as the Channel between England and France , and
that between England and Ireland.
Some Collections of Water lie within Land
and thefe are:
1. A Lake is a Collection of Water furrounded
with dry Land, without any vifible Communica-
tion with the Sea.
2. A River is a flowing Water arifing from a
Spring in fome high Land, and continually run-
ning in a Channel till it empties itfelf into fome
other River, or finally, into the Sea.
3- A Fountain is the Place where Water firff
arifes or fprings out of the Earth.
4. A Cataraß is a Precipice in the Channel of
a River, where the whole Body of a River falls
perpendicular from a great Height.
