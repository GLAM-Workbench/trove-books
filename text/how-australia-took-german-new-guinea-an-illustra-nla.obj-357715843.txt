HOW AUSTRALIA TOOK GERMAN NEW GUINEA
The capture of German New Guinea by the Australian squadron and
the first Australian Expeditionary Force marks a distinct stage in Aus-
tralian history. Australians had already fought side by side with their
British comrades in the Soudan, and in China during the “Boxer” dis-
turbances, and in the South African War had earned a reputation for
hard riding and hard fighting. But in all these cases the Australian
troops were part of a much larger force and under the direct orders
of British commanders. At Rabaul, and in New Guinea, they were not
so. For the first time Australia was given work to do with her own
men under her own officers, convoyed and helped by her own squadron ;
and the expedition thus marks, we may hope, the definite coherence of
six colonies into a nation which sooner or later will be able to accept
the responsibility for maintaining Imperial interests in the Pacific.
The existence of a state of war between Britain and Germany was
known in Australia on August 5. Volunteering immediately began
for an expeditionary force to be sent to Europe, and on August 9 it
was announced that a second force, vaguely destined “for the tropics,”
was to be mobilized at once under the command of Colonel W. Holmes,
D. 5.0., V.D., then in command of the sixth infantry brigade. Within
little more than a week this force, selected from the hardiest men of
the first batch of volunteers, had been organized, armed, uniformed,
equipped, and (so to speak) licked into some sort of shape, and on
August 18 it was embarked on the P. & O. Company’s steamer Berrima,
which in the same time had been fitted up, victualled, stored and com-
missioned as a transport.
On the 19th the Expedition left Sydney in the Berrima under sealed
orders. Off Moreton Bay it was convoyed, pausing en route to give
the troops a fortnight’s hard training. On September 2, under escort
of a detachment of the Australian fleet, the Expedition left the coast
of Queensland for Port Moresby; then, rounding the Louisiade group,
it was directed straight for the German headquarters in New Pomerania
(formerly called, and now again to be called, New Britain).
These headquarters were at Rabaul, a comparatively new settle-
ment on Simpsonshafen (otherwise Blanche Bay) in the northern end
of New Britain. Just outside the bay lies Herbertshohe, the former
German capital; and somewhere in its neighbourhood there was known
to be a wireless station which must be destroyed as soon as possible.


Australian warships had been into Simpsonshafen earlier in the cam-
paign, but had not discovered the station, though they had received
assurances from local officials that the island was surrendered. Act-
ing, apparently, on these assurances a small force under Commander
Beresford, R.N., was now landed at Herbertshohe, and another to the
east of it at Kabakaul, to locate and capture the station. But between
the two visits the German officials, acting under instructions from the
Acting-Governor, Dr. Haber, had begun to fortify the approaches to
the station, especially along the road from Kabakaul, entrenching a
position, lining the roadway with rifle-pits, and stationing native sharp-
shooters in the cocoanut palms alongside; so that the troops had to
fight their way for some hours under a sharp fire, and lost seven
men (including Lieutenant-Commander Elwell, R.N., an old King’s
School boy, and Captain Brian Pockley, A.A.M.C.) before they broke
the German resistance. In this affair the Naval Brigade under Captain
Stevenson rendered good service. The station itself was captured
and destroyed next day, Captain Travers, A.1.C., taking a conspicuous
part in this operation.
Meanwhile the Acting-Governor had retired from Rabaul to I oma,
ten miles inland from Herbertshohe; Rabaul itself, therefoie, was
surrendered on September 12 without serious resistance, and next day
the British flag was hoisted there and possession taken of the island
with due formalities. On the 14th, Toma, from which the Governor
had again retired, was occupied by troops under the command of
Coloned Watson; on the 15th Dr. Haber came down to Herbertshohe
to parley with the Brigadier —a parley which ended in surrender; and
on the 21st the remainder of the German forces, commanded by Captain
von Klewitz, marched into our lines and laid down their arms. Next
day a section of the Expeditionary Force left Simpsonshafen with the
Brigadier in the Berrima for Friedrich-Wilhelmshafen, the chief Ger-
man station in New Guinea proper; it was expected that this poit would
be defended, but no defence was in fact attempted, and on the 24th it
surrendered without delay.
Thus the first expedition carried out by Australian troops acting
by themselves, under their own officers, was brought to a successful
close, and the colony of German New Guinea successfully occupied
through the agency of an Australian force.
