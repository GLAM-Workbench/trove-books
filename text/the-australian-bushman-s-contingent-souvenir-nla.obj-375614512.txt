THE AUSTRALIAN BUSHMEN’S CONTINGENT.
One of the brightest pages in tne history of the colonial empire will be that whicn tells how a far-seeing few originated the
now famous Bushmen’s Contingent, and carried it into effect by their indomitable efforts. From the picturesque and practical
view points, nothing more effective has been done by these colonies than the formation of the contingent of 500 mounted bushmeu
in New South Wales. It is a true expression of the patriotism that has burned so fiercely in this colony. Australia will be specially
represented in the struggle in South Africa by this brigade, as specially as was the United States in the war with Spain by Roose-
veldt’s Rough Riders, and New South Wales takes her place, as mother colony, in contributing half of the total number of men
sent from the whole of the colonies.
The credit of the initiation of this great patriotic movement belongs to Major J. Randal Carey. Forcibly struck with the value
of the employment on the side of the British army in South Africa of a corps of back wood smien—hard, nervy, sinewy men who
can ride and shoot well, carry their own commissariat, and generally accustomed to “roughing it,” and who, from the practical point
of view, would be able to meet the crafty Boer at the irregular warfare he can play so well, and beat him at it, Major Carey
zealously set to work. The result is the equipment and dispatch to South Africa of a mounted contingent of 500 bushmen and 20
officers, amid the patriotic enthusiasm of, not only the colonies, but of millions of loyal British people all over the world.
At the outset, Major Carey considered that his best plan was to consult the wealthy men of Sydney. The first gentleman he
approached promised £3OOO (this was Mr. Samuel Hordern, who, however, did not wish his name to be published then). Mr. Walter
R. Hall, Potts Point, was next spoken to, and he promised £5OOO, and “more if required.” Success was, therefore, assured from
the beginning. Delighted by his initial response, Mr. Carey consulted the Premier on the subject, and Mr. Dyne promised that the
Government would do all it could to assist such a splendid movement. The assistance of the military authorities was also of-
fered, Major-General French promising to do all in his power. A decisive step was then taken. It was agreed that Mr. Carey,
Mr. Geoffrey Fairfax, and Mr. John See should act as joint treasurers for the fund. Aid was also promised from London, the Pre-
mier receiving the following cable message from the Agent-General:—“lt is proposed by Australasians here (London) to form a fund
for the pui’pose of equipping mounted bushmen from the colonies. They desire to know whether the Australasian Governments
would countenance such action. It is understood that the Imperial Government would accept the men. Agents-General for the
Australasian colonies request you please communicate this other Governments.”
An appeal for funds was then made, and there wasthe same enthusiastic response, six subscribers donating £1(5,000. They were: Mr.
W. R. Hall, £5000; Mr. S. M'Gaughey, M.L.0., £5000; Mr. S. Hordern, £3000; Miss Eadith Walker, Uralla, £1000; John Fairfax and Sons,
£1000; Mr. P. H. Osborne. £IOOO. Definite shape was given to the movement at a public meeting held at the Pastoral Union Office
on January 4. At this meeting, which was marked by genuine patriotic enthusiasm throughout, it was announced that un
asked for, the sum of £10,015 had been subscribed, and this amount was swelled to £10,840 at the meeting. Amongst the important
decisions arrived at was one that the contingent should be distinctly Federal in its character, and that invitations should be extended
to the citizens of the other colonies to join with those of New South Wales in providing the necessary men, horses, and funds. The
intention then was to send a combined Australian brigade of 500 men, but it was subsequently decided that New South Wales


should equip a contingent of 500 men from this colony alone. The other colonies, are, of course, sending men, and the whole
contingent from Australia will number about 1100 men. An executive committee consisting of the following was formed at this
meeting:—Mr. J. R. Carey, Mr. Geoffrey Fairfax, Mr. John See (treasurers ex officio), Sir George Dlbbs, Sir William P. Manning, Mr.
H. E. Kater, M.L.C., Dr. Mackellar, M.L.C., Messrs. M. J. Macnamara, J. T. Walker, Philip H. Morton, George J. Cohen, W. Kilgour,
and Mr. J. M. Atkinson (hon. secretary ex officio).
The enthusiasm increased after this meeting, and offers of men, money, and horses poured in. To the list of subscribers, Mr.
W. E. Sparkes, who gave £2OOO, has to be added. The patriotic spirit in which the people throughout the colony, in town and country,
responded, even exceeded anticpations. Personally greater enthusiasm was displayed over the formation of the contingent than any
of the previous contingents. One reason for this is simple. The usefulness of such a force in aiding the British army appealed to
everyone. In a fortnight a camp had been formed at Rand wick, hundreds of men were being tested as shots and riders, 700 men alto-
gether having been enrolled, and nearly £22,000 had been subscribed. To-day over £30,000 has been subscribed in this colony, and
a sum of £5OOO is expected from the London fund, £lOOO from Mr. A. A. Daugar, and over £4OOO worth of horses has been subscribed.
This is the kind of patriotism that tells.
Every day swelled the number of volunteers, who came from all parts of the colony, many with swags around them, and all
with big, brave hearts, and eager to measure their skill and strength with the enemy. The stream of volunteers was apparently
brooklike, and the roll soon numbered about 1100 men. At this point the executive committee discontinued enrolment. The supply
was much greater than the demand, and a formidable task was presented in the selection of the 500 men. Prom such an army of
sinewy men of the forest, all so eager to serve, the difficulty was not so much in choosing, but in deciding who must stand out. The
qualifications of every man as to his bush experience, his riding and shooting ability, and character, had to be carefully gone into,
and, finally, amid the delight of the chosen, and heart-burning of the rejected, the selection of the whole contingent of 500 men,
divided into four squadrons, was completed. And the result is the banding together of a body of men, which will not only be a pic-
turesque, typical representative one of Australia, but one of invaluable worth. For scouting and outpost work the Bush Brigade
should prove one of the most valuable adjuncts of the British Army. The equipment of this force at the sole expense of the
people is a splendid monument of their loyalty to the motherland.
The Bushmen’s Contingent was dispatched from Sydney on the 28th February, 1900, by the S.S. Atlautian and the S.S. Maple
more, amidst a scene of the utmost enthusiasm, the course of the procession being lined with scores of thousands of spectators,
and decorated with myriads of flags. As the horses were being put on board the men were drawn up in line on the jetty, and
patriotic speeches were made by Sir P. M. Darley (Lieutenant-Governor), the Hon. W. J. Lyne (Premier), the Hon. John See (Colonial
Secretary), and Major J. Randal Carey. As an indication of the warm feeling felt by the public for the Contingent, numerous gifts
were made of articles for the personal comfort and luxury of the troops while on the voyage and on duty in the field.
