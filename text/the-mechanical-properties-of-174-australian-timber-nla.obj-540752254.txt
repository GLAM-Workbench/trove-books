FOREWORD
When C.5.1.R.0. Bulletin No. 279, “The Mechanical Properties of
Australian, New Guinea, and Other Timbers” by Stewart and Kloot, was
published in 1957, it was intended that further data on the mechanical
properties of additional timbers would be issued from time to time in the
form of supplements to the original publication. However, the demand
for Bulletin No. 279 proved so great that by the time sufficient data
became available for the first supplement, the bulletin was out of print.
This technological paper has therefore been prepared as a re-edition
of Bulletin No. 279 but with two major changes. Firstly, the list of timbers
has been considerably extended, and secondly, data are given only for
Australian timbers. The information on New Guinea timbers given in the
Bulletin is currently being augmented and will be published separately
at a later date.
The data presented here were obtained by the C.5.1.R.0. Division
of Forest Products over many years and represent only species for which
the results are believed to give at least a reasonable indication of their
properties. To derive the maximum benefit from the information, the
species averages should always be interpreted with due regard to the
accompanying variability statistics.


THE MECHANICAL PROPERTIES OF 174 AUSTRALIAN TIMBERS
By E. Bolza* and N. H. Kloot*
[Manuscript received August 28, 1962]
Summary
Mechanical properties are tabulated for 174 Australian timbers. The text com-
prises explanatory notes to facilitate use of the tables by wood technologists.
I. Introduction
Although from time to time the mechanical properties of several Australian
timbers have been reported in detail (see Appendix I), until recent years a consolidated
table of the mechanical properties of local timbers has not been published except
by early investigators, Warren (1892), Julius (1906), Nangle (1913), Mann (1921),
and others, whose work in this field pre-dated internationally recognized timber
testing methods.
Over the past 30 years the Division of Forest Products, C.5.1.R.0., has, by
conducting tests to accepted timber testing specifications, accumulated considerable
data on a large number of Australian timbers. This information provided here in
tabular form is intended as a convenient reference for those working in the field of
wood technology. No attempt has been made to explain in great detail remarks per-
taining to the methods of sampling and testing as these will be well understood by
timber research workers, who will also appreciate the limits of the use of the data
presented.
With the exception of the two species coachwood (Ceratopetalum apetalum)
and bollywood (Litsea reticulata), which were intensively investigated by the Division
of Wood Technology, N.S.W. Forestry Commission, the information given in the
tables has been derived only from tests conducted by this Division.
It is not intended that this publication should replace the more detailed reports
of the properties of individual species. These will continue to be published from time
to time.
11. Explanation of Tables
(a) Species
Where possible the common name shown for each timber is the Standard
Trade Common Name as given in the Australian Standard Nomenclature of Aus-
tralian Timbers (Standards Association of Australia 1940). For timbers not listed in
this Standard or for which a change in name has occurred since the Standard was
published, the common names have been taken from standard reference books.
To enable important species which have undergone recent changes in botanical
name to be readily recognized, synonyms are also given. Where several species have
been grouped under one reference name because of the difficulty in separating them

by botanical identification, the names of the individual species so grouped have been
listed.
As the timbers in Table 1 are listed in alphabetical order of common name, a
cross-reference from botanical names is provided in Table 2.
(b) Origin of Timber Tested
For a number of the timbers, the sampling has covered substantially the whole
of the geographical range of availability. Thus alpine ash has been sampled from
Tasmania, Victoria, and New South Wales. On the other hand, white cypress pine,
silver quandong, and many others have so far been sampled from only one or other
of the States in which these timbers grow.
Extreme care should be exercised in making use of the data given for the
plantation-grown loblolly, slash, and radiata pines. Current studies of these and
other exotic pines indicate that a marked increase in strength properties occurs
throughout the early years of the tree’s life so that the strength of a particular piece
of wood is dependent on its distance from the pith, and the average strength of all
pieces cut from the tree depends very much on the tree’s age. The loblolly and slash
pine logs sampled were 16 years old. The radiata pine sampling contained trees
of the 30- and 40-year age class from South Australia, together with trees ranging
from 18 to 33 years old from Victoria. Radiata pine is grown in every Australian
State but there is evidence to suggest that site differences, not necessarily measured
by site quality, which is an indicator of rate of growth, affect strength properties.
Thus the figures given should not be taken as directly indicating the properties of this
species as grown in, say, New South Wales or Western Australia. However, irres-
pective of any other consideration, that is of age class, site quality, or any other
growth factor, reasonably accurate comparisons between different samples of defect-
free radiata pine (and any of the other pines) may be made on a strength/weight
basis (unpublished data).
(c) Moisture Condition
The “green” condition refers to timber of which the moisture condition was
above the “intersection point” (Wilson 1932), i.e. in the region in which the mech-
anical properties of wood are independent of the moisture content. Only a few of the
timbers listed were reconditioned after drying and of these only mountain ash and
alpine ash are considered likely to have had their properties significantly affected by
the reconditioning process. Thus, except for these two timbers, the values given at
12% moisture content for all the timbers may be taken as for dried and unrecon-
ditioned material.
In the dry condition, each species mean has been adjusted to the equivalent
value at 12% moisture content, either by means of the Madison exponential formula
(Wilson 1932) or by percentage corrections similar to those listed by Markwardt and
Wilson (1935). Impact test results have not in general, for reasons given by Kloot
(1954), been so adjusted, but the figures may be taken as being sufficiently indicative
for practical purposes of these properties at 12% moisture content.


(d) Number of Trees Tested
Only those species for which information has been obtained from three or more
trees in either the green or dry condition have been listed. The greatest number of
trees represented in the averages for the various properties is given and serves mainly
to indicate the magnitude of the sampling. To allow the statistics to be used effec-
tively, the number of trees represented in each individual average is given in the body
of the tables as explained below. Culling of specimens with visual defects such as
cross grain, knots, seasoning checks, and gum veins reduced the number of results
available for averaging in some properties. Specimens having defects not readily
visible to the eye, such as compression wood and tension wood, have not in general
been culled.
A number of the timbers listed were systematically sampled, taking specimens
from bark to pith as required by the American specification (American Society for
Testing Materials 1952). Of recent years, however, the sampling has been modified
to substantially one specimen of each type selected at random from each tree accord-
ing to the procedure recommended by Pearson (1952).
(e) Density
In a number of instances, the density values in Table 1 will be found to differ
somewhat from those given by Kingston and Risdon (1961). A check on these
differences has indicated that they may be attributed almost entirely to differences
in size of sample, i.e. the number of trees tested. Where the density value alone is
required, the result from either source based on the larger number of trees should
be taken as the better estimate. When, however, the relationship between strength
and density is of prime consideration, only the values for density given here in
Table 1 should be used.
Whilst the meaning of air-dry density seldom causes any difficulty, the term
basic density, and the relationship between it and the density of wood in the green
condition, is quite often not understood. Basic density is the weight of oven-dry
wood per unit volume measured in the green condition. If the moisture content of the
green wood is known, the actual density in this condition is simply given by the
relationship:

each represents the weight of a cubic foot of wood at 12% moisture content.

For a number of species, mainly from Queensland, the amount of test material

111. General
With few exceptions, the timbers listed were tested in controlled temperature
conditions of 70 ± 3°F. It is not considered likely that the errors involved in the
data for those timbers tested prior to the institution of temperature control in this
laboratory are serious. Nevertheless, in making comparisons between the values
given in the tables and data from other sources, consideration should be given to the
temperature effect (Sulzberger 1943) in view of its size and the lack of temperature
control in some laboratories.
