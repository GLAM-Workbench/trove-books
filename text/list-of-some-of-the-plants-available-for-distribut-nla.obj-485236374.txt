NOTE CAREFULLY:
NOMENCLATURE. —Every possible precaution is taken to ensure plants
being true to name.
As the Society receives seeds and plants from Collectors all over the world
—with new importations—it is impossible to guarantee all plants true to name.
Everything sent out is plainly labelled.
PACKING .—The greatest care and most approved methods are adopted
for packing.
Unless specific instructions are received, best judgment wnl be used as to
route, etc., so as to ensure safe delivery and least cost.
delivery .—After delivering to carrier or company, no responsibility is
accepted for loss or injury to plants which have been carefully packed and shipped,
but the Society will do everything possible for protection and recovery of any
member’s property.
fi y || If any mistakes are made in filling requisitions, the same will be rectified,
provided prompt notifications be sent on receipt of plants-,
IN ORDERING—State whether substitution will be permitted.
When no instructions accompany the requisition, the Overseer will be at
liberty to replace with other sorts as nearly similar as possible —should the
varieties ordered be exhausted.
A list of “ second choice ” accompanying each requisition would be appre-
ciated, and on occasions of shortage of any variety will facilitate the filling of
requisition.
Members not acquainted with the different varieties may do well in leaving
the selection to the Overseer, who will then send only such as are known to be
adapted to the special locality. M
The Society will always be glad to aid members in their selection, when so
desired, and will, upon request, furnish additional information as to the adapt-
ability or desirability of particular varieties.
TREATMENT ON ARRIVAL. —The plants should be removed from the
packages as soon as possible after receipt. If in the least wilted place in a shallow
pan of luke warm water for say half-an-hour, with packing still about roots.
Plant as soon as possible; until planted, leave no roots exposed to air|;
always keep covered with damp straw or wet bagging.
Mulch on surface, and shade as may be required.
Thoroughly water—one thorough drenching is worth many small applications.
EXTRA FINE SPECIMENS. —The Society has generally on hand a limited
number of extra size trees, palms, etc., grown in large pots, but not in sufficient
quantity for ordinary distribution. Anything in large pots will be quoted upon
specific inquiry as to kinds.
RECORDS OF PROGRESS. —A very important part of the Society’s
operations being to secure distribution of varieties into the localities best suited
for the same, members in receipt of plants are requested to forward particulars
as to their success or otherwise with the plants received, the mode of treatment
planting, cultivation, and any other pertinent information will be welcomed,
and will assist the Society in the object it has in view.
Records of a failure are sometimes .more instructive than of a success.
INFORMATION —concerning the whole of the Society’s operations will be
supplied upon application to the Secretary, who will define the various varieties
of plants available and due to members, so that, selection can be made.
For membership, etc., see last page.

Queensland Acclimatisation Society.
BOWEN PARK, BRISBANE.
The Council desire to bring under your notice the benefits derived from
joining the Society.
1. Members are allowed free access to the Society’s Grounds, both at Bowen
Park, and at Lawn ton (N.C. Line).
2. The Overseer, Mr. James Mitchell, and other officers of the Society will
give any information desired, with the benefit of their extended experience.
3. Members may draw plants, etc., up to the value of their subscriptions.
4. Members receive copies of the Society’s publications.
The Subscription is £1 Is. per annum in advance, starting from any date.
The Secretary will enrol members on receipt of accompanying form, filled in,
at any time.
Remittance should be by cheque, with exchange added; money order or
postal note.
The Council will be pleased if you will extend your co-operation in increasing
the membership by making the objects and advantages known amongst your
friends, thus enabling the useful work of the Society to be extended.
The Society is always willing to arrange for exchanges of plants or seeds,
and is desirous at the present time of entering into correspondence with
Associations, firms and individuals, no matter where situated, with a view to
receiving plants or seeds, for which other plants and seeds, or other exchanges,
will be supplied.
